You finished JoSAA. Maybe you got a seat you are okay with but not thrilled about. Maybe you got nothing at all. Then you hear about **CSAB special rounds** and a fresh wave of hope — and confusion — arrives.

This guide explains what the **CSAB special round 2026** is, how it differs from JoSAA, who should and should not participate, and the one risk that catches students off guard: the chance of losing the JoSAA seat you already hold. Read it before you register.

> **The honest short version:** CSAB is a real second chance at NIT, IIIT and GFTI seats — but only worth joining if a better seat is genuinely possible *and* you understand exactly what you are risking. Confirm every rule on the official CSAB notice for the current year.

## What is CSAB?

CSAB (Central Seat Allocation Board) runs the **special rounds** that happen *after* JoSAA wraps up. Across its 5–6 rounds, JoSAA allocates seats in all the IITs, NITs, IIITs and GFTIs. But some seats always end up vacant — a student gets allotted, then does not report, withdraws, or chooses a different path. Those empty seats need to be filled.

That is CSAB's job. It pools the **leftover NIT, IIIT and GFTI seats** and offers them in fresh special rounds, typically two. One thing to fix in your head straight away: **CSAB does not handle IIT seats.** IIT vacancies are dealt with separately by the IITs. So if your only goal is an IIT, CSAB is not your route.

CSAB also widens the gate slightly. Some candidates who never got a JoSAA seat — including those who could not participate fully earlier — get a chance here, alongside students who already hold a JoSAA seat and want to try for something better.

## How CSAB differs from JoSAA

This is where most of the confusion lives. CSAB is not "JoSAA round 7." It is a separate process with its own registration and its own choice list. Here is the side-by-side.

| Feature | JoSAA | CSAB Special Rounds |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What it covers | IITs, NITs, IIITs, GFTIs | NITs, IIITs, GFTIs only (no IITs) |
| When it runs | First, 5–6 rounds | After JoSAA ends, usually 2 rounds |
| Registration | One registration | Fresh, separate registration |
| Choice filling | One locked list carries across rounds | New choice list, filled afresh |
| Seats offered | All available seats | Only seats left vacant after JoSAA |
| Float/freeze/slide | Available across rounds | Limited options; rules differ |
| Fees | Seat Acceptance Fee | Separate CSAB processing/seat fees |

The practical takeaways:

- **You must register again.** Your JoSAA login does not automatically carry you into CSAB. Miss the registration window and you are out, full stop.
- **You fill choices from scratch.** The list you so carefully built for JoSAA does not transfer. Order this new list by what you genuinely want, top to bottom — the same discipline applies.
- **The seat pool is smaller and unpredictable.** You are picking from leftovers. A great branch at a top NIT may appear, or it may not. There is no way to know in advance exactly what will open up.

## The float/freeze nuance — and the seat you might lose

This is the part to slow down for.

If you **already hold a JoSAA seat** and you enter CSAB hoping to upgrade, you are usually allowed to keep that seat as a fallback while you try for something better. In most years this works through a **float**-style option: you stay registered for an upgrade, and if you get one, you move up; if you do not, you generally retain your existing seat.

But — and this is the catch — the exact behaviour **changes from year to year**, and some scenarios can cost you your JoSAA seat:

- If you **withdraw** your JoSAA seat before or during CSAB, you have given it up. There is no taking it back.
- In some years, certain registration or freeze actions in CSAB are treated as accepting whatever CSAB gives you, which can mean releasing your original seat.
- If you get an upgrade in CSAB, your old seat is automatically released to the pool — that is the whole point, but make sure the upgrade is one you actually want before you trigger it.

The float/freeze/slide logic that governs all of this is worth understanding properly. We break it down in [float, freeze and slide in JoSAA](/blog/float-freeze-slide-josaa) — read it before you touch CSAB.

> **Do not rely on this article for the exact rule.** CSAB publishes detailed "business rules" each year. The wording around retaining versus releasing your JoSAA seat is precisely the part that gets revised. Read the current year's official document, twice, before you register or freeze anything.

## Who should participate?

CSAB makes clear sense for these candidates:

- **You got no JoSAA seat at all.** You have little to lose and a genuine shot at an NIT, IIIT or GFTI seat. This is exactly who CSAB is built for.
- **You hold a JoSAA seat you would happily trade up from.** A clearly defined upgrade — a better branch, a closer-to-home institute, a stronger NIT — is on your wish list, and you understand the float mechanics.
- **You are flexible on branch or location.** Vacancies favour students willing to take what opens up. Rigid preferences are harder to satisfy from a leftover pool.

## Who should sit it out?

CSAB is not for everyone. Skip it if:

- **Your JoSAA seat already meets your goals.** Chasing a marginal "better" seat and risking confusion — or your existing seat — is not worth it. A good seat in hand beats a maybe.
- **Your only target was an IIT.** CSAB has no IIT seats. There is nothing here for you on that front.
- **You cannot track deadlines and rules carefully.** CSAB moves fast and is unforgiving about missed windows and misread rules. If you cannot give it real attention, the downside risk rises.

## Key steps and dates

Exact dates for 2026 will be on the official CSAB notice — do not trust forwarded screenshots. But the flow is predictable:

1. **Watch for the CSAB notification.** It appears soon after the final JoSAA round closes. Bookmark the official CSAB site and check daily during that gap.
2. **Register afresh.** Pay the processing fee, confirm your details, and complete registration within the window.
3. **Fill and lock your choice list.** Build it from scratch, ordered by true preference. Lock before the deadline.
4. **See your allotment and decide.** Based on that year's rules, choose to accept, freeze, float for a further upgrade, or withdraw — knowing what each does to any JoSAA seat you hold.
5. **Pay and report.** Complete fee payment and document verification for the seat you settle on.

Keep your documents ready the same way you did for JoSAA — there is no extra grace for "I couldn't find my certificate" during these tight windows.

## Coordinate with your private college backups

Here is the timing trap. CSAB runs late in the season. By then, many **private and state colleges** have already taken your admission deposit and may be deep into their own admission process. If you are holding a paid private seat as insurance while you chase a CSAB upgrade, understand the money at stake.

Private college **deposit forfeiture** rules are often strict, and the refund you get for leaving late can be far less than you paid. Before you gamble a private backup on a CSAB long shot, read [private college deposit forfeiture](/blog/private-college-deposit-forfeiture) and do the maths. Sometimes the safer, cheaper move is to stop chasing and commit.

A simple decision frame:

- **CSAB upside:** a possible upgrade to a better NIT, IIIT or GFTI seat.
- **CSAB downside:** wasted fees, missed private deadlines, and — in the wrong scenario — losing the JoSAA seat you held.

Weigh both honestly. If the realistic upgrade is small and the risk to a seat you already like is real, the disciplined answer is often to walk away satisfied.

## A quick reality check on rank and vacancies

CSAB fills *leftover* seats. Whether a good option appears depends on what other students vacated and how your rank stacks up against those specific seats. Nobody — no website, no counsellor — can promise you a particular seat will open or that you will get it. Be wary of anyone who does; that is exactly the kind of false certainty we warn about, and a guaranteed-seat claim is a red flag, not a service.
