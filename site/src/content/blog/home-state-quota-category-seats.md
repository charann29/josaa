Two students with almost the same JEE rank can land in very different colleges — not because one filled choices better, but because of **which seat pools they were eligible for**. Home state, category and gender all quietly reshape what your rank can actually reach in JoSAA.

This guide explains those pools in plain English: home-state quota, category seats, female-supernumerary seats and PwD seats. Get these right and you fill your list with confidence. Get them wrong and you either leave seats on the table or — worse — put a seat on your list that you can't actually take.

> One rule before we start: the exact percentages, formats and validity dates change every year and differ by institute. We state the general, well-known rules here. Always confirm the current numbers in the official JoSAA information brochure before you lock anything.

## Home-State (HS) vs Other-State (OS) quota

This is the pool that surprises the most families.

At **NITs and most GFTIs**, the seats in each state are split into two buckets:

- **Home-State (HS) quota** — for candidates whose qualifying state matches the state where the NIT is located.
- **Other-State (OS) quota** — for everyone else applying to that NIT.

Why it matters: the HS cutoff is usually **more relaxed** for the home-state student than the OS cutoff at the same NIT. So your home state effectively unlocks a friendlier set of closing ranks at *your* state's NIT.

A student from Tamil Nadu, for example, competes in the HS pool at NIT Tiruchirappalli but in the OS pool at, say, NIT Warangal. The same rank can be "Safe" in one and "Dream" in the other — purely because of the pool.

### How your home state is decided

Your home state is normally based on where you passed your qualifying examination (Class 12 or equivalent), not simply where you live. There are special rules for some Union Territories and for institutes in places without their own NIT. Because the mapping has edge cases, **confirm your home-state status in the brochure** rather than assuming.

### IITs and IIITs work differently

This trips people up: **IITs generally do not have a home-state quota.** They admit on an all-India basis. IIITs broadly follow the same all-India approach. So the HS/OS strategy is mostly an **NIT and GFTI** game.

The practical takeaway: don't expect a home-state advantage at an IIT. Plan your IIT choices on all-India closing ranks, and use the HS edge where it actually exists — at your state's NIT and GFTIs.

## Category seats: OPEN, OBC-NCL, EWS, SC, ST

JoSAA reserves seats by category in line with central reservation policy. The categories you'll see include:

- **OPEN** (general / unreserved)
- **OBC-NCL** (Other Backward Classes — Non-Creamy Layer)
- **EWS** (Economically Weaker Section)
- **SC** (Scheduled Caste)
- **ST** (Scheduled Tribe)

### How category changes your reachable colleges

When you register, you get a **Common Rank List (CRL)** rank and, if you belong to a reserved category, a **category rank** as well. JoSAA compares you against the **closing rank of your category** for each seat — not just the open closing rank.

In practice this means a reserved-category student is often considered against more relaxed closing ranks, which can make colleges and branches reachable that the open list would put out of range. The key thing to understand: **your category rank, not just your CRL, drives your Safe / Likely / Dream tagging.** Tag your list against the closing ranks for *your* category and pool, year by year — never against a single year's open cutoff.

A note on honesty: a reserved seat is your right, but it still must match the actual closing ranks. We never promise a particular college, and we never sell numbers — we read the real closing ranks and tell you the truth.

## Female-supernumerary seats

To improve gender diversity, most participating institutes add **female-supernumerary seats** — extra seats created specifically for women candidates. "Supernumerary" simply means **added on top of** the existing seats, not carved out of them.

What this means for an eligible woman candidate:

- You are considered for **both** the regular (gender-neutral) pool **and** the female-supernumerary pool.
- You do **not** give up a regular seat by being eligible for a female seat. The system places you in the best option you qualify for.
- The female closing rank for a branch is often more relaxed than the gender-neutral one, which can widen what you can reach.

You don't apply separately for these — eligibility is built in. But it's worth understanding so you tag your list realistically and don't underestimate your options. The exact share of female-supernumerary seats is set per the current rules, so check the brochure for specifics.

## PwD seats — and the "restriction-safety" rule

JoSAA also reserves seats for **Persons with Disabilities (PwD)** across categories, with a horizontal reservation. If you have a valid disability certificate in the prescribed format, you compete for PwD seats within your category.

Here is the rule that matters most and is the easiest to get wrong:

> **A seat you physically cannot take should never be on your list.**

Some programmes have specific physical or medical requirements. If a branch's requirements mean you would not be able to study or work in it safely, putting it high on your list can win you a seat you then can't accept — wasting the round and risking your other options. This is what we call **restriction-safety**: every choice on your list must be a seat you could genuinely join.

This applies to everyone, not only PwD candidates — but it deserves extra care here. Read the eligibility and any medical-standard notes for each branch in the brochure, and only list seats that are truly open to you.

## The certificates you'll need

Each pool needs the right certificate, in the right format, issued within the required window. Missing or wrongly-formatted certificates are one of the most common reasons reporting goes wrong — so get these ready **before** counselling, not on deadline day.

| Pool / claim | Certificate needed | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| Home-State (HS) | Proof of qualifying state (usually Class 12 details / domicile as specified) | Home state is by qualifying exam location, not residence — confirm in the brochure |
| OBC-NCL | OBC Non-Creamy-Layer certificate, central-government format | Must usually be issued in the required recent financial year |
| EWS | EWS certificate, central-government format | Usually needs to be of the required recent financial year |
| SC / ST | Caste certificate in the prescribed format | Must match the central list and prescribed format |
| PwD | Disability certificate from a recognised authority | Must meet the prescribed format and percentage requirements |
| Female-supernumerary | No separate certificate — eligibility is automatic | Just ensure your registration details are correct |

The exact formats, issuing authorities and validity dates are set in the brochure each year and can change — treat the table above as a checklist of *what to arrange*, then verify the *exact* requirements. ## How all this changes your Safe / Likely / Dream tagging

Quota and category aren't side notes — they decide what "Safe" even means for you. A clean way to think about it:

1. **Fix your pools first.** Home state, category and gender eligibility together define which closing ranks you should be compared against.
2. **Tag against the right column.** A branch might be Dream on the open all-India list but Likely in your category's HS pool at your state's NIT. Tag it on *your* pool, across multiple years — not one year's open cutoff.
3. **Order by what you want.** JoSAA gives you the highest choice in your list that you qualify for, so the order still matters most. Once your pools are set, build the order using our [choice-filling order guide](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026).
4. **Apply restriction-safety.** Strike any seat you couldn't actually take.
