Every JEE Main result season, the same confusion shows up in our inboxes: "I got 98.6 percentile — what's my rank, and which NIT can I get?" The honest answer has two parts. First, percentile and rank are not the same thing. Second, the percentile is only a stepping stone — the number that actually drives your college is your **rank**.

This guide explains how the conversion really works: what NTA normalisation does, why two students with identical marks can land on different percentiles, and how to estimate your rank from your percentile yourself. We'll keep the maths simple and clearly mark every example as illustrative.

> **One thing up front:** the formula below gives an *estimate*. Your official rank comes only from NTA after both sessions are normalised and merged. Use the estimate to plan, not to celebrate or panic.

## Percentile vs rank: the core difference

These two numbers measure different things.

- **Percentile** answers: "What percentage of candidates scored at or below me?" It runs from 0 to 100. A 99 percentile means you did as well as or better than 99% of the candidates. The topper of each session gets 100.
- **Rank (All India Rank, or AIR/CRL)** answers: "What is my exact position in the merit list?" Rank 1 is the best. Lower rank is better.

The key contrast: percentile is **relative to the size of the pool**, while rank is your **precise spot in line**. A 99 percentile sounds like a small gap from 100, but with around 10–12 lakh candidates, that 1% gap can be 10,000–12,000 students sitting ahead of you.

| Feature | Percentile | Rank (AIR / CRL) |
|---|---|---|
| Range | 0 to 100 | 1 to total candidates |
| Better value | Higher | Lower |
| What it measures | Your standing vs the pool | Your exact merit position |
| Depends on candidate count? | No (always 0–100) | Yes |
| Used by JoSAA / colleges? | No | Yes |

That last row is the one to remember. **No college predictor, no JoSAA closing-rank list, no cutoff sheet works in percentiles.** They all work in ranks. So percentile is where you start; rank is where decisions happen.

## How NTA normalisation works (and why it exists)

JEE Main is held in two sessions (January and April), and each session runs across multiple shifts on multiple days. That means different students attempt **different question papers**. No two papers are exactly equal in difficulty — one shift's maths section might be brutal, another's gentler.

If NTA simply added up raw marks across all shifts, students who happened to get an easier paper would have an unfair edge. **Normalisation fixes this.** Instead of comparing raw marks directly, NTA converts each student's raw score into a **percentile within their own session/shift**, using the formula:

> Percentile = (Number of candidates in the session with raw score equal to or below you ÷ Total candidates in that session) × 100

Because the percentile is calculated *within* each session, it cancels out the difficulty difference between papers. A student at the top of a hard shift and a student at the top of an easy shift both land near 100 percentile, even if their raw marks differ.

This is also why **percentile is reported to many decimal places** (like 99.7642318). With lakhs of candidates, the extra decimals are needed to separate people who would otherwise tie.

### Why two students with the same marks can get different percentiles

This is the part that surprises most families. Suppose two students both score 180/300 — one in January, one in April.

- In January, maybe the paper was harder, so 180 put the student ahead of more people in that session. Higher percentile.
- In April, maybe the paper was easier, so more students also crossed 180. The same 180 now beats a smaller share of the pool. Lower percentile.

Same raw marks, different percentiles — entirely because of which session and how it was normalised. This is working as intended: percentile reflects your *standing*, not your raw score. (Illustrative; actual gaps vary by year and session.)

When both sessions are done, NTA takes each student's **best of the two NTA scores** (percentiles) and merges everyone into a single list to produce the final All India Rank.

## Estimating your rank from your percentile

Here is the method. It is an approximation, but a useful one for planning.

> **Estimated Rank ≈ ((100 − Your Percentile) ÷ 100) × Total Number of Candidates**

The logic is simple: your percentile tells you the percentage of people *below* you, so `(100 − percentile)` is roughly the percentage *above* you — and that percentage of the total pool is how many people are ranked ahead of you.

### Illustrative example (approximate — varies by year/session)

Let's assume **12,00,000 (12 lakh)** candidates appeared. Plugging in a 99 percentile:

> Estimated Rank ≈ ((100 − 99) ÷ 100) × 12,00,000 = (1 ÷ 100) × 12,00,000 = **about 12,000**

So a 99 percentile is roughly rank 12,000 in this scenario — not the "almost rank 1" feeling the number suggests.

**Important caveats:**

- The total candidate count changes every year. If 14 lakh appear instead of 12 lakh, the same percentile maps to a *higher* (worse) rank number.
- The official rank uses your **best NTA score across both sessions**, after full normalisation. The formula can't replicate that exactly.
- Treat the output as a **ballpark**, accurate to maybe a few thousand ranks — fine for planning, not for precise cutoff decisions.

### Illustrative percentile to approximate rank table

The table below assumes **12 lakh candidates** purely to show the *shape* of the relationship. Do not read these as your real rank.

| Percentile | Approx. AIR (at 12 lakh candidates) |
|---|---|
| 100 | ~1 (topper) |
| 99.9 | ~1,200 |
| 99.5 | ~6,000 |
| 99 | ~12,000 |
| 98 | ~24,000 |
| 97 | ~36,000 |
| 95 | ~60,000 |
| 90 | ~1,20,000 |

Notice how steep it is at the top. Going from 99.9 to 99 — a difference of less than one percentile point — moves you by roughly 10,000 ranks in this example. That steepness is exactly why percentile alone is a poor planning tool.

## CRL vs category rank: you have more than one rank

NTA doesn't give you a single rank — it gives you several. The two that matter most are:

- **CRL (Common Rank List / All India Rank):** your rank among *all* candidates, ignoring category. This is the headline rank.
- **Category rank:** your rank *within your category* (for example, OBC-NCL, SC, ST, EWS, or PwD). This counts only candidates in your category.

Your category rank is always better (lower number) than or equal to your CRL, because the category pool is smaller. During JoSAA seat allocation, **general/open seats are filled by CRL**, while **reserved category seats are filled by category rank**. So a reserved-category student effectively gets two shots — qualifying through open seats on CRL, or through category seats on category rank, whichever helps.

This is why, when you plan, you should know *both* your CRL and your category rank, and check closing ranks against the right one for each seat type. We cover how category and home-state quotas interact in our guide on category seats and home-state quota.

## Why you must plan with rank, not percentile

Three reasons, all practical:

1. **Everything official is in ranks.** JoSAA opening and closing ranks, branch cutoffs, and every credible predictor speak in CRL and category rank. A percentile can't be matched against them directly.
2. **Percentile hides the true gap.** As the table shows, a "high" percentile can still mean tens of thousands of students ahead of you. Rank tells you the unvarnished truth.
3. **Percentile is volatile across years.** The same percentile maps to different ranks depending on how many candidates appear. Last year's closing *ranks* are far more stable to plan against than any percentile rumour.

So the workflow is: get your percentile → convert to an approximate rank → compare that rank against last year's closing ranks → build your choice list.

## Using your rank for choice filling

Once you have an estimated rank, here's how to turn it into a sensible JoSAA list:

- **Pull last year's closing ranks** for the branches and colleges you care about (and the year before, if you can — trends matter).
- **Sort your options into three buckets** relative to your estimated rank: ambitious (closing rank better than yours, a stretch), realistic (around your rank), and safe (closing rank clearly worse than yours, near-certain).
- **Order by genuine preference, top to bottom** — not by rank. JoSAA always gives you the highest choice in your list you qualify for, so your number 1 should be what you actually want most. Our [choice-filling order guide](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026) walks through this.
- **Decide branch vs college early.** Whether you'd rather have a stronger branch at a slightly lower-ranked NIT, or a top NIT with a less-preferred branch, changes your whole list. See [branch vs college](/blog/branch-vs-college-jee).
- **Leave margin for the estimate.** Because your converted rank is approximate, include a few extra safe options so you're not caught out if your real rank lands a little lower than expected.

## A quick recap

- **Percentile** = your standing relative to the pool (0–100). **Rank** = your exact merit position (lower is better).
- **Normalisation** converts marks to percentiles *within each session* so different paper difficulties are fair — which is why same marks can give different percentiles.
- **Estimate rank** with `((100 − percentile) ÷ 100) × total candidates` — but treat it as a ballpark.
- Know your **CRL and category rank**, and plan everything against **ranks**, not percentiles.
