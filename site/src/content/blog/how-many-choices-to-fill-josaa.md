You've ordered your list, you're staring at the JoSAA portal, and the question hits: **how many choices am I supposed to fill?** Twenty? Fifty? A hundred? Friends quote different numbers, and some warn that filling "too many" will somehow drag you down.

Let's settle it. There is no correct number to memorise — but there is a correct **rule**, and once you understand it, the right count for *your* situation becomes obvious.

> **The one rule:** Fill every option you would genuinely be willing to join, in the order you'd want them — and never stop before you've added a safe option you'd actually accept.

## There is no cap — and no required number

JoSAA does not set a maximum on how many choices you can fill. Your choice list is built from every programme across all participating institutes — 23 IITs, 31 NITs, IIEST, the IIITs and the GFTIs. That's thousands of branch-plus-institute combinations. You could, in theory, add an enormous number.

There's also no meaningful minimum beyond "at least one valid locked choice to submit." So the portal won't stop you whether you fill 5 or 105. That freedom is exactly why students get anxious — the system gives you no hint about what's "enough."

The answer doesn't come from the portal. It comes from your own preferences. New to the whole process? Start with the [complete JoSAA counselling guide](/blog/josaa-counselling-2026-complete-guide) and come back here.

## Why 40–100+ choices is completely normal

When students sit down and honestly list every institute and branch they'd be happy at — across all four institute types, including home-state and other-state NITs — the list naturally grows long. A serious aspirant who is open on location often ends up with **40 to over 100 choices**, and that's healthy, not excessive.

Here's why the number climbs:

- **Branch combinations multiply.** If you'd accept Computer Science, then Electronics, then Mechanical at a given NIT, that's three choices at one institute alone.
- **Many institutes qualify.** Open to several NITs and IIITs across the country? Each adds a cluster of choices.
- **Home-state and other-state seats are separate listings.** [Home-state quota and category seats](/blog/home-state-quota-category-seats) appear as distinct options, expanding the list further.
- **Reaches, likely options and safe options all belong on one list.** A complete Dream → Likely → Safe spread is long by design.

A long list is not "spray and pray." Every entry should still be something you'd accept. The length comes from genuinely having many acceptable options — not from padding.

## The real danger is filling too few

The myth says filling more hurts you. The truth is the opposite: **filling too few is what actually loses students seats.**

JoSAA runs multiple rounds, and closing ranks shift between rounds and between years. Your rank sits in a band, not on a fixed line. If your list is short and a round moves against you — closing ranks tighten, your preferred branch fills early — none of your listed options may be within reach that round. The result: **no seat allotted, and a round gone that you can't get back.**

A short list with no cushion is a bet that everything goes your way. It usually mostly does — until the one round it doesn't, and that's the round that decides your year. The students who get caught out are rarely careless; they're often the confident ones who assumed their preferred options were "safe enough" and didn't bother extending the list. This is one of the quietest, costliest [choice-filling mistakes](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-mistakes) students make.

The fix is simple and free: a **safe tail** — a handful of options at the bottom of your list whose recent closing ranks sit comfortably below your rank, that you would genuinely attend. The safe tail is the single most important part of "how many." A list can be short up top, but it should never end without a real safety net.

## Quantity vs intent: it's fine to fill fewer — with one condition

Long lists are common, but you are not *obligated* to fill a hundred. If you honestly would not attend an option, leaving it off is correct. Filling choices you'd never join just to hit a number is pointless — you'd never get pushed that far down anyway, and if you somehow were, you'd be holding a seat you don't want.

So the count should follow your **intent**, not a target:

- Truly only want a specific set of institutes or one branch? A shorter list is fine.
- Open to more? Your list should reflect that and grow accordingly.

The one non-negotiable: **whatever the length, keep a safe tail you'd actually accept.** "I'll fill fewer because I only want the top ones" is fine — right up until a bad round leaves you with nothing. If the rest of your list is short, your safe options matter *more*, not less.

## Myth-bust: adding choices does NOT reduce your chances at the top

Let's kill this one for good, because it drives bad decisions.

JoSAA's allocation is strict: in each round, you are given the **highest-ranked choice in your list that you qualify for** at that moment. The algorithm walks your list from the top down and stops at the first option you're eligible for.

This means:

- Your 1st choice is evaluated first, every round. Always.
- A choice lower in your list is only ever reached if you *didn't* qualify for everything above it.
- Adding a 50th, 80th or 100th choice **cannot** affect whether you get your 1st. Lower choices are pure insurance.

There is zero penalty for a long list. The only thing that changes your odds at a particular choice is your **rank** and that choice's **closing rank** — never how many other options sit below it. So the safe-but-undesirable option you put at the very bottom can never "steal" the top seat from you; it just waits there in case you need it.

The real lever isn't the count — it's the **order**. Get the sequence right and the length takes care of itself. That's covered in depth in the [choice-filling order guide](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026).

## How many choices for your profile?

Use this as a starting frame, not a hard rule. The right number is whatever honestly captures your acceptable options plus a safe tail.

| Your situation | Suggested approach | Rough count |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Open on branch and location; want the best possible outcome | Full Dream → Likely → Safe list across all institute types | 60–100+ |
| Set on one or two branches, flexible on institute | Every institute offering those branches, ordered best to worst, plus a safe tail | 30–60 |
| Only want a specific tier (e.g. top NITs/IITs) | List your true targets, then *still* add a genuine safe tail you'd attend | 20–40 |
| One branch only, will not study anything else | All institutes for that branch, top to bottom, including safe ones below your rank | 15–40 |
| High AIR, confident of a top reach | Short reach-heavy list — but never skip a few safe backups | 15–30 |

Notice every row ends the same way: with a safe tail. Whatever your profile, that's the part you can't omit.

## How this connects to ordering and the safe tail

"How many" and "in what order" are the same decision viewed from two angles. You build the list by **intent and order**, and the count is simply the result:

1. **Top:** your reaches — options above your rank band. Free shots; list them all.
2. **Middle:** your likely options — within your rank band.
3. **Bottom (the safe tail):** options with closing ranks comfortably below your rank, that you'd genuinely accept. This is your guarantee against a bad round.

Build all three honestly and your number lands wherever it lands — and it'll be correct, because every entry earned its place.
