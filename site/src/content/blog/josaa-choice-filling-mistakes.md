Two students with the exact same JEE rank, same category, same home state can end up in completely different colleges. The reason is rarely luck. It is almost always **how they filled their choice list** — and the small, quiet mistakes one of them made without realising.

JoSAA does not warn you when you are about to make a costly error. It simply runs your list through the allotment engine and gives you a result. By the time you notice, the round is over and there is no undo.

This is the list of **JoSAA choice-filling mistakes** that cost students good seats every year — and the exact fix for each. If you are starting from scratch, read the [complete JoSAA 2026 guide](/blog/josaa-counselling-2026-complete-guide) first; this post is the "what not to do" companion.

> The one rule behind almost every mistake here: JoSAA gives you the **highest choice in your list that you qualify for**. Order by what you want, not by anything else.

## The mistakes, and how to fix each one

### 1. Filling institute-wise instead of preference-wise

This is the most common structural error. Students pick a favourite institute, fill *all* its branches, then move to the next institute and fill all of those, and so on. It feels organised. It is wrong.

JoSAA does not understand "institutes" as groups — it only reads your single ordered list, row by row. If you put every branch of NIT Trichy above your first choice at NIT Surathkal, you are telling the system you would rather have a branch you dislike at Trichy than the branch you love at Surathkal.

**The fix:** order **purely by preference**. For every two rows, ask only one question — "if I could have just one of these, which?" That answer goes higher. The full method is in [JoSAA choice filling order 2026](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026).

### 2. Filling too few choices, or no safe tail

Some students fill 15 choices and stop. Others fill 80 dream choices and nothing safe. Both can end a round with **no seat**.

JoSAA lets you fill a large number of choices, and they cost you nothing — an unfilled or short list only narrows your safety net. The danger is a list that runs out of options before it reaches anything your rank can actually secure.

**The fix:** build the list as **Dream → Likely → Safe**, and make sure the bottom genuinely is safe (a seat your rank cleared comfortably across recent years, not just last year). See [how many choices to fill in JoSAA](/blog/how-many-choices-to-fill-josaa) for sizing this properly.

### 3. Not locking before the deadline

You can save choices and come back later. But a *saved* list is not a *locked* list. Students assume "I'll lock tomorrow," lose track of the deadline, or hit a slow portal in the final hour.

If you never lock, JoSAA auto-locks your **last saved list** when the window closes. If that draft was half-edited or mis-ordered, that is the list that runs — permanently.

**The fix:** finalise early, **lock yourself** a day before the deadline, and save the locked confirmation as a screenshot/PDF. Never rely on auto-lock.

### 4. Ordering by your rank instead of your preference

A subtle but expensive one. Students look at last year's cutoffs and arrange the list so "achievable" colleges come first and "tough" ones go lower — basically sorting by probability.

That defeats the entire design. Dream choices at the top **cannot hurt you**: if you do not qualify, the engine moves down. But put a safe-but-less-wanted college above your dream, and the moment you qualify for the safe one, the engine stops there. You never even get considered for the better option.

**The fix:** rank by desire, not by odds. Reaches at the very top, then likely, then safe. Use cutoffs to *label* each row Dream/Likely/Safe — not to *order* them.

### 5. Ignoring home-state quota and category benefits

Many students fill their list as if only the open All-India pool exists. They skip the seats where their **home-state quota**, **category**, gender-neutral vs female pool, or supernumerary seats give them a real edge — and end up with worse options than they qualified for.

**The fix:** map your eligibility before you order anything. Home-state seats at your state NITs, and category/PwD seats you are entitled to, often unlock colleges that look out of reach in the open pool. Read [home-state quota and category seats](/blog/home-state-quota-category-seats) so you do not leave seats on the table.

### 6. Blindly trusting one free college predictor

A free predictor says "you'll get X," so the student builds the whole list around that one output — often from a tool whose real product is collecting your phone number.

Predictors use different data years, different assumptions, and rarely show you their working. One number is not a strategy.

**The fix:** treat any single prediction as a hint, not a verdict. Check a seat against the **actual closing rank in each of the last few years**, not an average. And be wary of who you hand your details to — see [why free predictors spam you, and who to trust](/blog/free-college-predictor-spam-trust).

### 7. Misunderstanding Float, Freeze and Slide

Choice filling does not end at Round 1. After each result you must choose **Float, Freeze or Slide** on the seat you were allotted — and this trips up even careful students. Freeze when you meant to Float and you forfeit an upgrade you were in line for. Float carelessly and you may move to something you did not actually prefer.

**The fix:** decide your float/freeze/slide rule *before* results, based on your locked preferences. Plain-English breakdown here: [Float, Freeze and Slide explained](/blog/float-freeze-slide-josaa).

### 8. Missing reporting and document deadlines

You can win a great seat in the allotment and still **lose it** — by missing the reporting window or showing up with a document mismatch (name spelling, category certificate format, income proof validity).

**The fix:** keep every document ready before results, and diarise each reporting and fee deadline the moment it is announced. The [documents and reporting checklist](/blog/josaa-documents-reporting-checklist) covers exactly what to keep and the errors that get seats cancelled.

### 9. Paying private-college deposits carelessly

While JoSAA runs, private and deemed colleges push you to "book your seat now" with a non-refundable deposit. Students pay out of fear, then get a better JoSAA seat and lose the money — or feel trapped into the private seat.

**The fix:** read the refund terms in writing before paying anything, and never let fear of missing out drive a five-figure deposit. See [private-college deposit forfeiture](/blog/private-college-deposit-forfeiture) before you pay.

### 10. Falling for "guaranteed seat" agents

Every season, agents promise a "confirmed seat" in a specific top college through management or special quota — for a fee. JoSAA and CSAB seats are allotted by a transparent rank-and-list engine. **No one can guarantee a specific government seat.**

**The fix:** if someone promises a guaranteed seat, walk away. Learn the warning signs in [guaranteed-seat scams in JoSAA](/blog/guaranteed-seat-scams-josaa). Genuine guidance helps you *build a better list* — it never sells you a seat.

## How to sanity-check your list before you lock

Run this quick pass before the deadline:

- **Top down, no surprises.** Read your list from row 1. If any row makes you think "wait, I'd rather have the one below it," fix the order.
- **A real safe tail.** Confirm the last several choices are seats your rank cleared comfortably across recent years.
- **Quota seats present.** Your home-state and category options actually appear where they should.
- **Locked, not just saved.** You see the locked confirmation, and you have a screenshot.

Most damage in JoSAA is quiet — no error message, just a slightly worse result than you deserved. A careful list and a calm lock prevent almost all of it.
