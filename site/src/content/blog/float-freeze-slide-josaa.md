You got a seat in a JoSAA round. Now the portal asks you to pick one of three options — **Float**, **Freeze** or **Slide** — and the wording is just confusing enough that thousands of students pick the wrong one every year. Some freeze a seat they actually wanted to upgrade from. Others float without understanding what is protected and what is not.

This guide explains all three in plain English, tells you exactly **when** to pick each, and shows what happens to your seat in the next round. There is a decision table you can use as a quick reference during your own counselling.

> **The short version:** Freeze = accept and stop. Float = accept but keep chasing any higher choice. Slide = accept but keep chasing only a higher branch in the same institute.

## The three options, defined clearly

When a seat is allotted to you in a JoSAA round, your choice list does the work of *finding* the seat. These three options decide what happens **next** with the seat you just got.

### Freeze — accept and stop

**Freeze** means: "I am happy with this seat. Do not consider me for anything else."

- You accept the allotted seat.
- You are removed from all further rounds.
- Your seat is final once you report and pay the seat acceptance fee.

This is the right choice when the seat you got is genuinely the one you want — there is nothing higher on your list you would still take. The cost of freezing is finality: there is no upgrade after this.

### Float — accept but stay in the race for anything higher

**Float** means: "I accept this seat for now, but if any choice ranked *above* it on my list opens up, give it to me."

- You accept the allotted seat as a safety net.
- You stay active in the next round.
- The system tries to give you a **higher** choice from your list.
- If nothing higher opens up, you keep exactly the seat you already hold.

The key point students miss: floating **never moves you down**. The seat you currently hold becomes your floor. You either stay there or move up — never below.

### Slide — accept but stay in the race for a higher branch in the same institute

**Slide** means: "I accept this seat, but only upgrade me if a more-preferred branch in *this same institute* opens up."

- You accept the allotted seat.
- You stay active in the next round, but only for higher-preference branches **within the same institute** you were allotted.
- You will not be moved to a different institute, even if a higher choice there opens.
- If no better branch in that institute opens, you keep your current seat.

Slide is the narrow version of float. It is for students who love the college and only want to improve the branch there.

## What happens to your seat in the next round

This is where the confusion usually lives. Here is the outcome under each option, assuming you reported and paid on time:

| Option | Stays in next round? | Can move up to a higher choice? | Can move to a different institute? | Can you lose / drop below current seat? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Freeze | No | No | No | No (seat is final) |
| Float | Yes | Yes — any higher choice on your list | Yes | No — current seat is your floor |
| Slide | Yes | Yes — but only a higher branch in the same institute | No | No — current seat is your floor |

The single most important row to internalise: under **both float and slide, you can never drop below the seat you already hold.** The allotted seat is protected. The only thing at risk is missing a deadline.

## Exactly when to pick each — the decision table

Match your situation to the option. This is the part to keep open during counselling.

| Your situation | Choose |
| --- | --- |
| You got your top choice, or a seat you are fully happy with | **Freeze** |
| You are done and do not want the stress of more rounds | **Freeze** |
| You got a decent seat but still want a shot at higher choices anywhere | **Float** |
| Your dream branch/college is ranked above your current seat and still possible | **Float** |
| You love the institute you got but want a better branch in it | **Slide** |
| You are fine with the college but the branch is your only concern | **Slide** |
| You got an early-round seat and want to see if Round 2/3 opens something better | **Float** |
| You are unsure — but you might want an upgrade | **Float** (it is the safest non-final choice) |

When genuinely in doubt and you have *any* appetite to move up, float is the lowest-regret pick: it protects your current seat and keeps the widest set of upgrades alive. You can always freeze in a later round once you are satisfied.

## The two mistakes that cost students their upgrade

### Mistake 1: Freezing when you wanted to move up

This is the most painful one. A student gets a seat in Round 1, feels relieved, and clicks Freeze — only to realise later that a choice ranked higher on their list opened up in Round 2 and they were no longer eligible for it. **Freeze ends the game.** If there is anything above your current seat you would still accept, do not freeze.

### Mistake 2: Floating but misunderstanding the floor

The opposite worry also trips people up. Some students avoid floating because they think they might *lose* their seat or be pushed into a worse option. That does not happen. Floating keeps your current seat as a guaranteed floor; the system only ever tries to lift you to a **higher** choice. Understanding this removes most of the fear around floating.

There is also a quieter version: floating but having a messy choice list, so the "higher choices" the system can give you are not actually ones you want. Float only helps if the choices ranked above your current seat are choices you genuinely prefer.

## How this ties to your choice order and mock allotment

Float and slide can only ever pull you **up your own list**. So the upgrades available to you are decided entirely by how you ordered your choices in the first place. If a branch you would happily take is sitting *below* your current seat, floating will never reach it — order matters.

- Put choices strictly in the order of what you actually want, top to bottom. Your [choice-filling order](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026) is what defines every possible float/slide upgrade.
- Use the [mock seat allotment](/blog/josaa-mock-seat-allotment-explained) to preview where your list is likely to land before you lock. It is the best tool for sanity-checking whether floating is worth it for you.
- Remember the upgrade ladder is fixed once you lock. Float and slide work the ladder; they do not build it.

## Deadlines and locking each round

Picking float, freeze or slide is not a one-time decision — it repeats after every round in which you hold a seat.

- After each round, if a seat is allotted to you, you must **report online, pay the seat acceptance fee, and choose your option** within that round's window.
- The deadlines are tight, often a couple of days. Confirm exact dates on josaa.nic.in every cycle.
- Missing the deadline can mean **losing the seat entirely** — this is the real risk, not the float/slide choice itself.
- Between rounds you also get a window to edit and re-lock your choice list; this is separate from the float/freeze/slide step on your allotted seat.

A simple rhythm to follow each round: check your allotment, decide freeze vs upgrade, report and pay, select your option, confirm it saved — well before the clock runs out.

## When float runs out: thinking ahead to CSAB

If you float through all JoSAA rounds and still want to try for a better NIT-system seat, the [CSAB special rounds](/blog/csab-special-rounds-2026) run afterwards for leftover seats. Whether to hold your JoSAA seat and also try CSAB is its own decision — but knowing it exists can change whether you freeze early or keep floating.

## Quick recap

- **Freeze** = accept and stop. No upgrade after this. Use it when you are genuinely happy.
- **Float** = accept but chase any higher choice on your whole list. Your current seat is a protected floor.
- **Slide** = accept but chase only a higher branch in the same institute. Narrower than float.
- You **cannot** drop below your allotted seat by floating or sliding — you can only stay or move up.
- The real danger is missing a reporting or payment deadline, not the option you pick.
