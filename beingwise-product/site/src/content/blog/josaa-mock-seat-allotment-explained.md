You have filled your choices on josaa.nic.in, and now you see a line that says **Mock Seat Allotment**. A college name appears. Your heart either jumps or sinks.

Before you react, read this. The mock result is one of the most useful — and most misread — features in JoSAA. Used well, it helps you fix a weak list before it is too late. Misread, it pushes students into panic edits that cost them a better seat.

This is the plain-English guide to what mock allotment is, how to read it, how much to trust it, and exactly what to change before you lock.

## What is JoSAA mock seat allotment?

Mock seat allotment is a **free preview** of where you might land, generated by JoSAA before choice locking closes. It is a trial run of the real allotment algorithm.

Two things feed into it:

- **Your current choice list** — the colleges and branches you have entered and in what order.
- **All registrations and choices entered so far** by every other candidate, with their ranks and categories.

JoSAA runs the same matching logic it will use for the real Round 1 — your rank, your category, home-state quota, and the seat matrix — and shows you the highest choice in your list you would currently get.

The key word is *currently*. It is a snapshot of a moving picture.

> Mock allotment is a mirror, not a ticket. It shows you how your list looks right now against the field as it stands right now.

For how the full process fits together, see our [complete JoSAA 2026 counselling guide](/blog/josaa-counselling-2026-complete-guide).

## How many mock rounds are there?

JoSAA usually publishes **one or two mock allotment results** during the choice-filling window, before locking closes.

- The **first mock** comes when a good chunk of candidates have registered. Early data, so treat it loosely.
- The **second mock** (if released) comes closer to the deadline, with more candidates settled in. It tends to be a bit more stable.

Both are checkpoints. Neither is your final seat. After each mock, you can keep editing and reordering your list, then lock.

## How to read your mock allotment

Open the result and look at it calmly. Ask these questions in order.

### 1. Which choice number did you get?

If you were allotted **choice number 7 out of 30**, that tells you something good: six choices above it were out of reach right now, and the rest below are your backups. If you got your **last choice (choice 30)**, that is a warning — your safety net is thin.

### 2. Is it above or below your honest expectation?

Compare the mock seat to what you genuinely expected from your rank. A mild surprise in either direction is normal. A big gap is worth understanding before you edit.

### 3. Is there a gap in your list?

The most common problem mock reveals is a **missing middle**. Many students fill dream colleges at the top and one or two random safe options at the bottom, with nothing in between. If the mock seat sits at the very bottom, your middle is probably empty.

### 4. Did you get *no* seat at all?

No mock seat usually means one of three things: your list is **too short**, **too top-heavy** (only ambitious choices), or you are **outside the realistic range** of everything you listed. All three are fixable — see the actions below.

For the order itself, our [choice-filling order guide](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026) walks through how to sequence dream, realistic and safe options correctly.

## How accurate is mock allotment — and why does it shift?

Mock allotment is a **strong signal, not a promise**. It is calculated from incomplete and changing data, so it moves between the mock and the real result for honest reasons:

- **More candidates keep registering.** Every new high-rank registrant in your category can push you down a notch.
- **Everyone is editing choices.** Other students reorder and add options right up to the deadline, changing the competition for each seat.
- **You may edit too.** The moment you change your list, the mock that produced your earlier preview no longer applies.
- **Quota and category seats are tight.** Home-state and reserved seats can swing borderline cases either way.

A simple way to think about it:

- **Deep inside a college's range** — your mock seat there is fairly reliable.
- **Clearly outside a college's range** — that "no seat" is also fairly reliable.
- **Right on the borderline** — this is where mock shifts the most. Do not bank on a borderline mock seat, and do not give up on a borderline miss.

This is exactly why mock is not a [guaranteed final seat](/blog/josaa-counselling-2026-complete-guide). Anyone promising you a fixed outcome from a mock result is misreading the tool.

## Mock result to action: a quick table

Use this as your decision sheet. Match your mock result to the right response — and notice how often the answer is "small tweaks," not "rewrite everything."

| Mock result | What it likely means | What to do before locking |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Got a top choice (choice 1–3) | List is realistic; you are well placed | Leave the order alone. Optionally add one or two safe options at the bottom for insurance |
| Got a middle choice | Healthy spread of options | Keep it. Re-check that everything above your mock seat is something you truly prefer |
| Got your last/bottom choice | Safety net is thin; middle may be empty | Add realistic mid-range options between your dream picks and your safe ones |
| Got a seat you do not actually want | A choice is ranked higher than your true preference | Reorder by genuine preference; move or delete that option |
| Got no seat | List too short, too top-heavy, or out of range | Add solid realistic and safe choices below your ambitious ones; do not delete the dream picks unless you want to |
| Got a surprisingly good seat | Field is currently favourable to you | Do not over-trust it. Keep stronger preferences above it; lock a balanced list |

## What to change after a disappointing mock — without over-reacting

A bad mock result triggers two wrong instincts: **panic** (rewrite the whole list) and **denial** (change nothing and hope). Both lose seats. Here is the measured approach.

### Do this

- **Fix the gaps, not the whole list.** Add realistic and safe options where they are missing. Most disappointing mocks are a coverage problem, not an ordering problem.
- **Keep your preference order honest.** Always list colleges and branches in the order you actually want them, top to bottom. JoSAA gives you the highest choice you qualify for, so order is everything.
- **Lengthen a short list.** If you only filled a handful of choices, add more. There is rarely a downside to a longer, well-ordered list.
- **Add a genuine safety layer.** A few options clearly within reach at the bottom protect you across rounds.

### Do not do this

- **Do not delete your dream choices** just because the mock skipped them. They cost you nothing sitting at the top. You only ever get a *lower* choice if a higher one is out of reach — so a dream pick at the top can never pull you down.
- **Do not chase the mock seat to the top.** Promoting the exact college the mock gave you, above things you prefer more, is a classic mistake.
- **Do not rewrite everything off one mock round.** The data is still moving. A calm edit beats a full overhaul.

## Mock allotment and float / freeze / slide

A quick but important clarification: mock allotment happens **before** locking and the real rounds. The **Float, Freeze, Slide** decision comes **after** you receive a real allotted seat in an actual round — it is how you tell JoSAA whether to keep trying for something better or hold what you have.

So mock does not ask you to float, freeze or slide. It only helps you build a list strong enough that, when the real rounds begin, your float/freeze/slide choices are good ones. If those terms are new, read our [float, freeze and slide guide](/blog/float-freeze-slide-josaa) before Round 1.

## The deadline pressure: fix it before you lock

Here is the part students underestimate. The mock result is only useful if you **act on it before choice locking closes**. Once you lock — or once the window auto-locks your last saved list — that list is final for Round 1.

A simple pre-lock routine:

1. **Read the mock** using the four questions above.
2. **Match it** to the action table.
3. **Make small, deliberate edits** — add coverage, fix order, lengthen if short.
4. **Re-save and, if a second mock runs, recheck.**
5. **Lock with time to spare.** Do not wait for the final hour; portals are slowest and most error-prone then.

If the second mock looks healthier than the first, your edits worked. If it does not, revisit coverage once more — but resist the urge to gut the list.

## It is a preview, not a verdict

Let us say it plainly one more time: **the mock seat is not your final seat.** It is a free, honest preview that exists so you can catch a weak list while you still have time to fix it. Treat it as a coach pointing at the gaps, not a judge reading a sentence.

- A great mock means: lock a balanced list and breathe.
- A poor mock means: add coverage, keep your order honest, and lock early.
- A borderline mock means: do not bet the house on it either way.
