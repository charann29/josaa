Every JEE Main result season, the same confusion lands in our inboxes at BeingWise: "I got 98.6 percentile, what is my rank, and which NIT can I get?" The honest answer has two parts. First, percentile and rank are not the same thing. Second, the percentile is only a stepping stone. The number that actually drives your college is your **rank**.

This guide explains how the conversion really works in plain English: what NTA normalisation does, why two students with identical marks can land on different percentiles, and how to estimate your rank from your percentile yourself. We will keep the maths simple and clearly mark every example as approximate and illustrative.

> One thing up front: the formula below gives an estimate. Your official rank comes only from NTA after both sessions are normalised and merged. Use the estimate to plan, not to celebrate or panic.

## Percentile and Rank Are Two Different Things

These two numbers measure different things, and mixing them up is the single biggest mistake families make.

- **Percentile** answers: "What percentage of candidates scored at or below me?" It runs from 0 to 100. A 99 percentile means you did as well as or better than 99 percent of candidates. The topper of each session gets 100.
- **Rank**, the All India Rank or CRL, answers: "What is my exact position in the merit list?" Rank 1 is the best. A lower rank number is better.

The key contrast: percentile is **relative to the size of the pool**, while rank is your **precise spot in line**. A 99 percentile sounds like a tiny gap from 100, but with around 10 to 12 lakh candidates, that 1 percent gap can be 10,000 to 12,000 students sitting ahead of you.

| Feature | Percentile | Rank (AIR / CRL) |
|---|---|---|
| Range | 0 to 100 | 1 to total candidates |
| Better value | Higher | Lower |
| What it measures | Your standing vs the pool | Your exact merit position |
| Depends on candidate count? | No, always 0 to 100 | Yes |
| Used by JoSAA and colleges? | No | Yes |

That last row is the one to remember. **No college predictor, no JoSAA closing-rank list, and no cutoff sheet works in percentiles.** They all work in ranks. So percentile is where you start; rank is where decisions actually happen.

## Why NTA Uses Percentile and Normalisation

JEE Main is held in two sessions, January and April, and each session runs across multiple shifts on multiple days. That means different students attempt **different question papers**. No two papers are exactly equal in difficulty. One shift's maths section might be brutal, another's gentler.

If NTA simply added up raw marks across all shifts, students who happened to get an easier paper would have an unfair edge. **Normalisation fixes this.**

### What normalisation actually does

Instead of comparing raw marks directly, NTA converts each student's raw score into a **percentile within their own session and shift**, using this idea:

> Percentile = (Number of candidates in your session with a raw score equal to or below yours divided by Total candidates in that session) times 100

Because the percentile is calculated within each session, it cancels out the difficulty difference between papers. A student at the top of a hard shift and a student at the top of an easy shift both land near 100 percentile, even if their raw marks differ.

This is also why **percentile is reported to many decimal places**, like 99.7642318. With lakhs of candidates, the extra decimals are needed to separate people who would otherwise tie.

### Why the same marks can give different percentiles

This part surprises most families. Suppose two students both score 180 out of 300, one in January and one in April.

- In January, maybe the paper was harder, so 180 put the student ahead of more people in that session. Higher percentile.
- In April, maybe the paper was easier, so more students also crossed 180. The same 180 now beats a smaller share of the pool. Lower percentile.

Same raw marks, different percentiles, entirely because of which session and how it was normalised. This is working as intended: percentile reflects your standing, not your raw score. (Illustrative; actual gaps vary by year and session.)

When both sessions are done, NTA takes each student's **best of the two NTA scores** and merges everyone into a single list to produce the final All India Rank.

## How to Estimate Your Rank From Your Percentile

Here is the method. It is an approximation, but a useful one for early planning.

> Estimated Rank is approximately ((100 minus Your Percentile) divided by 100) times Total Number of Candidates

The logic is simple. Your percentile tells you the percentage of people below you, so (100 minus percentile) is roughly the percentage above you, and that percentage of the total pool is how many people are ranked ahead of you.

### A clearly-labelled illustrative example

Let us **assume 12,00,000 (12 lakh) candidates appeared. This number is illustrative only.** Plugging in a 99 percentile:

> Estimated Rank is approximately ((100 minus 99) divided by 100) times 12,00,000 = (1 divided by 100) times 12,00,000 = **about 12,000**

So a 99 percentile is roughly rank 12,000 in this scenario, not the "almost rank 1" feeling the number suggests.

Important caveats:

- The total candidate count changes every year. If 14 lakh appear instead of 12 lakh, the same percentile maps to a higher, that is worse, rank number.
- The official rank uses your best NTA score across both sessions, after full normalisation. The formula cannot replicate that exactly.
- Treat the output as a ballpark, fine for planning, not for precise cutoff decisions.

## CRL vs Category Rank: You Have More Than One Rank

NTA does not give you a single rank. It gives you several. The two that matter most are:

- **CRL, the Common Rank List or All India Rank:** your rank among all candidates, ignoring category. This is the headline rank.
- **Category rank:** your rank within your category, for example OBC-NCL, SC, ST, EWS, or PwD. This counts only candidates in your category.

Your category rank is always better, that is a lower number, than or equal to your CRL, because the category pool is smaller. During JoSAA seat allocation, **general and open seats are filled by CRL**, while **reserved category seats are filled by category rank**. So a reserved-category student effectively gets two routes: qualifying through open seats on CRL, or through category seats on category rank, whichever helps.

This is why, when you plan, you should know both your CRL and your category rank, and check closing ranks against the right one for each seat type.

## Why You Must Plan With Rank, Not Percentile

Three reasons, all practical:

1. **Everything official is in ranks.** JoSAA opening and closing ranks, branch cutoffs, and every credible predictor speak in CRL and category rank. A percentile cannot be matched against them directly.
2. **Percentile hides the true gap.** As the table shows, a high-looking percentile can still mean tens of thousands of students ahead of you. Rank tells you the unvarnished truth.
3. **Percentile is volatile across years.** The same percentile maps to different ranks depending on how many candidates appear. Last year's closing ranks are far more stable to plan against than any percentile rumour.

So the workflow is: get your percentile, convert it to an approximate rank, compare that rank against last year's closing ranks, then build your choice list.

### Turning your rank into a choice list

Once you have an estimated rank, here is how to turn it into a sensible JoSAA list:

- **Pull last year's closing ranks** for the branches and colleges you care about, and the year before if you can, because trends matter.
- **Sort your options into three buckets** relative to your estimated rank: ambitious, where the closing rank is better than yours and a stretch; realistic, around your rank; and safe, where the closing rank is clearly worse than yours and near-certain.
- **Order by genuine preference, top to bottom**, not by rank. JoSAA always gives you the highest choice in your list you qualify for, so your number one should be what you actually want most. Our [choice-filling order guide for 2026](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-order-2026) walks through this.
- **Decide branch vs college early.** Whether you would rather have a stronger branch at a slightly lower-ranked NIT, or a top NIT with a less-preferred branch, changes your whole list. See [branch vs college after JEE](/blog/branch-vs-college-jee).
- **Leave margin for the estimate.** Because your converted rank is approximate, include a few extra safe options so you are not caught out if your real rank lands a little lower than expected.

## Quick Recap

- **Percentile** is your standing relative to the pool, on a 0 to 100 scale. **Rank** is your exact merit position, where lower is better.
- **Normalisation** converts marks to percentiles within each session so different paper difficulties are fair, which is why the same marks can give different percentiles.
- **Estimate rank** with ((100 minus percentile) divided by 100) times total candidates, but treat it as a ballpark only.
- Know your **CRL and category rank**, and plan everything against ranks, not percentiles.
