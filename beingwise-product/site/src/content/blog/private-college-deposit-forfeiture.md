Every counselling season, families lose lakhs of rupees the same way. A student gets nervous while JoSAA is still running, books a seat at a private or deemed university "just to be safe," pays a hefty deposit, and then a later JoSAA round hands them the seat they actually wanted. They withdraw from the private college, ask for their money back, and discover the deposit was non-refundable.

This is one of the most expensive avoidable mistakes in the whole admission process. The good news: with a little planning around refund rules and JoSAA timing, you can keep a safety net and keep your money. This guide from BeingWise shows you how.

## Why students book a private or deemed backup during JoSAA

JoSAA runs over several weeks and multiple rounds. During that window you do not have a confirmed seat in hand. You have a locked choice list and a rank, but the actual allotment can shift round to round as candidates accept, float, slide, or withdraw. That uncertainty is uncomfortable, especially for borderline ranks.

So families look at a backup: a private or deemed university where you can confirm a seat now by paying a deposit. The well-known names students consider include BITS, VIT, SRM, and COMEDK-route colleges, among others. The logic is simple: "If JoSAA doesn't work out, at least I have something."

That instinct is reasonable. The problem is almost never the idea of a backup. The problem is the timing and the terms of the deposit.

## The forfeiture trap

Here is the trap, step by step:

1. JoSAA is still in progress. The student feels anxious.
2. They book a private or deemed seat and pay a deposit. Sometimes it is a token confirmation amount; sometimes it is a full year's fee.
3. The deposit is non-refundable, or only partly refundable, and the refund window has a deadline.
4. A later JoSAA round allots the student a seat they prefer.
5. They withdraw from the private college after the refund deadline has passed.
6. The college keeps the deposit. Lakhs gone.

The painful part is that step 6 is entirely predictable from step 3. The information needed to avoid it, the refund clause and the cut-off date, is usually printed on the college's own admission page. Students just do not read it before paying.

> The mistake is rarely "I booked a backup." It is "I booked a backup with a refund deadline that fell before the JoSAA round I was waiting on."

## How refund rules generally work

Refund policies differ from college to college, but most follow a recognisable pattern. The earlier you cancel, the more you get back; the later you cancel, the less you get back.

A typical structure looks like this. This is illustrative only, so always confirm the actual college's policy:

| When you withdraw | Typical refund outcome |
| --- | --- |
| Before admission closes or before a stated early cut-off | Most or all of the deposit refunded, minus a small processing amount |
| After the cut-off but before classes begin | Partial refund, with a meaningful chunk possibly forfeited |
| After classes begin or when the seat cannot be filled | Little to no refund |

Two things drive the outcome: the date you withdraw, and whether your vacated seat can be filled by someone on the waitlist. Many policies are more generous if the college can re-allot your seat.

The single most important variable is the cancellation or withdrawal deadline. Find that date. Write it down. Everything else in your backup plan revolves around it.

### The general UGC stance on refunds

The University Grants Commission has, for years, pushed colleges to refund fees to students who withdraw, rather than holding admissions hostage with non-refundable deposits. The general thrust of UGC's fee-refund guidance is that institutions should return fees, often keeping only a small, capped processing charge, when a student cancels, particularly within a stated window before the term begins.

That said, treat this as general guidance, not a guarantee for your specific case. Enforcement varies by institution type, by state regulations, and by how the college frames its "deposit" versus its "fee." A deemed university may interpret the rules differently from a state private university.

So the rule for you is simple: do not rely on UGC norms to bail you out. Verify the individual college's official, written refund policy before paying, and check the latest UGC notification yourself rather than trusting second-hand summaries. If they refund per UGC norms, wonderful. Plan as if they might not.

## How to time the backup against JoSAA round dates

This is the heart of avoiding forfeiture. You want your private college's refund deadline to fall after a JoSAA result you are willing to wait for. If you can wait for a round, get your JoSAA outcome, and then decide whether to withdraw, all while still inside the refund window, you keep both the safety net and your money.

Here is a planning table to map it out:

| Stage | What to do about the backup |
| --- | --- |
| Before JoSAA rounds begin | Shortlist one or two backup colleges. Note each one's deposit amount and refund deadline. Do not pay yet. |
| Early JoSAA rounds | If your allotment trend looks safe, hold off. You may not need to pay anything. |
| Mid rounds, outlook uncertain | If you must secure a backup, choose the one whose refund deadline is latest relative to the next JoSAA result. |
| After your decision round | If JoSAA gave you a seat you will accept, withdraw from the backup before its deadline and claim the refund. |
| CSAB special rounds | Remember these come after JoSAA and can still give NIT and IIIT seats. Factor them in before forfeiting anything. |

The mistake to avoid: paying a deposit whose refund deadline expires before the JoSAA round that would settle your decision. If the dates do not line up, that particular backup is a poor choice. Look for one with a later deadline, or accept that this deposit is genuinely at risk.

If you are relying on later rounds, understand how [CSAB special rounds 2026](/blog/csab-special-rounds-2026) fit into the timeline before you assume a paid backup is your only option.

## How to read a refund policy before you pay

Before any money leaves your account, find and read the written policy. You are looking for:

- The exact refund percentages at each cancellation stage.
- The cut-off dates that move you from one refund tier to the next.
- Whether the amount is called a "deposit," a "registration fee," or "tuition," because the wording can change the refund treatment.
- The processing or administrative charge the college keeps even on a full refund.
- The refund process and timeline: how you apply, what proof is needed, and how long the money takes to come back.
- Any condition tied to your seat being re-filled from a waitlist.

If the policy is vague, get clarity in writing, by email if needed, from the admissions office before paying. "We'll sort it out later" is not a refund policy.

## Before you pay a deposit: the checklist

Run through this every single time before paying:

- I have read the college's official written refund policy, not a salesperson's verbal version.
- I have noted the exact withdrawal or cancellation deadline and the refund at each stage.
- That deadline falls after the JoSAA round result I plan to wait for.
- I know the amount I would forfeit in the worst case, and I can accept that loss.
- I have CSAB special rounds factored into my timeline.
- I am paying through an official channel with a proper receipt, never cash to an "agent."
- No one has promised me a guaranteed seat in exchange for this payment.

If you can tick every box, a backup can be a sensible safety net. If you cannot, pause before paying.

## When a backup is actually worth it

A paid backup is worth it when:

- Your JoSAA outlook is genuinely uncertain and you would not be comfortable with the seats currently in reach.
- You can find a backup whose refund deadline falls after a JoSAA result you can wait for.
- The at-risk amount, the part you would forfeit in the worst case, is one you can accept losing.

A backup is probably not worth paying for when:

- Your rank comfortably reaches seats you would happily accept in JoSAA.
- CSAB special rounds are still ahead and likely to help you.
- The only backups available have tight, non-refundable deadlines that expire before your JoSAA decision point. In that case you are buying anxiety relief, not real insurance.

Often, the students who feel they need a backup actually need a better-ordered choice list. Many [JoSAA choice filling mistakes](/blog/josaa-choice-filling-mistakes) create exactly the anxiety that leads to a panic deposit. Fix the choice list first.

## A word on pressure and guarantees

Be especially wary of anyone pressuring you to pay immediately to "lock" a seat, or promising a guaranteed outcome. Real refund policies are written down and dated. Pressure tactics and guarantees are a red flag. JoSAA allotment is driven by your rank, category, and choices, and no agent controls it.
