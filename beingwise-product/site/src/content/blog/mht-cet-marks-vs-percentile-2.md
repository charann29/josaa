## How to calculate MHT CET Percentile?

The MHT CET percentile reflects your relative performance in comparison to other candidates, rather than your absolute scores. Understanding how to calculate a percentile is critical for analysing your findings using the MHT CET marks vs percentile vs rank framework. The whole step-by-step MHT CET Percentile computation process is shown below.

**Step 1: Determine Your Raw Score**

First, calculate the total marks you have secured in the MHT CET exam by checking out the official answer key provided by the authorities. It will be your raw score that will be used as the base for computing the percentile.

**Step 2: Find Out the Total Number of Candidates**

Now find out the total number of candidates who took part in the exam. It will help in calculating the percentile based on the overall performance of the candidate pool.

**Step 3: Find out how many candidates scored below you**.

Calculate the total number of students who scored equal to or fewer marks than your marks. This will show your performance against all candidates.

**Step 5: Perform the Calculation**

Take the total number of students scoring below your marks, divide this by the total number of candidates, and multiply this figure by 100.

**Step 6: Interpret Your Score**

The resultant figure from step five is your percentile score. A 95th percentile means that you scored higher than 95 percent of all candidates sitting for the exam.

**Step 7: Relate the Percentile to Your Rank**

After getting your percentile score, it will be used to calculate your rank in the merit list. The better your percentile, the higher your rank.

## MHT CET Marks vs Percentile: Factors to Consider

When looking at MHT CET marks vs percentile 2026, students must take into consideration more than just the marks secured to get an accurate estimation of percentile and ranks.

- **Exam Difficulty Level:** With an easy paper, students require high marks for a specific percentile, whereas with a difficult paper, low marks can fetch higher percentiles.
- **No. of Students Taking the Exam:**  A high number of candidates makes the race more competitive for securing a higher percentile.
- **Marks Distribution Among Students:** When many candidates have similar marks on the higher side, even small mark differences result in a huge percentile variance.
- **Normalization Procedure:** Due to the examination being conducted in multiple shifts, marks need to be normalized depending on exam difficulty.
- **Shift Differences:** Marks vary across different shifts due to differences in difficulty level, which can significantly impact percentiles.
- **Total Competitiveness of Exam**: The competitiveness of the year might lead to higher cut-offs for securing higher percentiles.
- **Compression of Top Marks**: For marks above 140, even a difference of 1 or 2 marks is bound to affect the percentile.
