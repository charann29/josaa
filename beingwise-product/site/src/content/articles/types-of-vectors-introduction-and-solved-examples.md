# Types of Vectors - Introduction and Solved Examples

Vectors are fundamental mathematical tools used to describe quantities that possess both a size (magnitude) and a specific orientation (direction). From understanding the path of a moving object to calculating forces, vectors play a crucial role in physics, engineering, and various real-world applications.

## Key Takeaways

*   Vectors represent quantities with both magnitude and direction, commonly shown as an arrow.
*   Understanding different types of vectors is essential for solving problems in vector algebra.
*   This topic is a significant part of Class 11 Mathematics and is frequently tested in competitive exams like JEE Main.
*   Between 2013 and 2023, the Joint Entrance Examination Main (JEE Main) included seven questions on vector types, highlighting its importance for aspiring engineers.

## What are Vectors?

A vector is a geometric entity characterized by its length, which signifies its magnitude, and its orientation in space, indicated by an arrow. Unlike scalar quantities (like temperature or mass) that only have magnitude, vectors provide a complete description by also specifying direction. For instance, while speed is a scalar, velocity is a vector because it includes both the speed and the direction of motion.

## Exploring Different Types of Vectors

Vectors are categorized based on their properties, such as their magnitude, direction, and how they relate to other vectors. Understanding these distinctions is key to mastering vector algebra.

### Zero or Null Vector

A zero vector, also known as a null vector, is a unique vector where its starting and ending points are the same. Consequently, its magnitude is zero, and its direction is undefined or indeterminate. It's often represented as $\overrightarrow{0}$ or by two identical points, like $\overrightarrow{AA}$.

### Unit Vector

A unit vector is a vector with a magnitude of exactly one unit. Its primary purpose is to indicate a specific direction. For any given vector $\vec{a}$, its unit vector in the same direction is denoted by $\hat{a}$ (read as "a cap") and is calculated by dividing the vector by its own magnitude:

$\hat{a} = \frac{\vec{a}}{|\vec{a}|}$

### Coinitial Vectors

Coinitial vectors are two or more vectors that all originate from the same starting point. Despite potentially having different magnitudes or directions, their common initial point defines them as coinitial.

### Collinear and Non-Collinear Vectors

Vectors are considered collinear (or parallel) if they lie on the same line or are parallel to the same line, regardless of their individual magnitudes or directions. Conversely, non-collinear vectors are those that do not act along the same line and are not parallel to each other. Non-collinear vectors are often referred to as independent vectors.

### Coplanar Vectors

A set of vectors is described as coplanar if they all lie within the same two-dimensional plane. It's important to note that any two vectors, whether parallel or not, will always be coplanar. However, three or more vectors may or may not be coplanar.

### Like and Unlike Vectors

Like vectors are parallel vectors that point in the exact same direction. Unlike vectors, while still parallel, point in opposite directions. Both categories are subsets of collinear vectors.

### Equal Vectors

Two vectors, say $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$, are deemed equal if they possess identical magnitudes and point in the exact same direction. Their initial positions in space do not affect their equality, as long as their magnitude and direction match.

### Negative of a Vector

The negative of a vector, say $-\vec{a}$, has the same magnitude as the original vector $\vec{a}$ but points in the completely opposite direction. For example, if $\vec{a}$ represents the vector from point A to point B ($\overrightarrow{AB}$), then $-\vec{a}$ would represent the vector from point B to point A ($\overrightarrow{BA}$).

## Solved Examples Based on Types of Vectors

Let's consider an example to solidify our understanding. If vector $\vec{P}$ has a magnitude of 5 units pointing East, and vector $\vec{Q}$ also has a magnitude of 5 units pointing East, then $\vec{P}$ and $\vec{Q}$ are equal vectors. If vector $\vec{R}$ has a magnitude of 5 units pointing West, then $\vec{R}$ is the negative of $\vec{P}$. Similarly, a vector from a point to itself would be a zero vector.

Understanding these vector types forms a crucial foundation for advanced topics in mathematics and physics, preparing students for competitive examinations and future academic pursuits.
