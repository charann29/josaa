The ellipse is a fundamental concept in coordinate geometry, a key area of Class 11 Mathematics. Understanding ellipses is vital not just for school exams but also for competitive entrance tests like JEE Main, SRMJEE, BITSAT, WBJEE, and BCECE.

## Key Takeaways

*   An ellipse is a special type of conic section, formed when a plane intersects a cone at an angle.
*   It can be defined as the set of all points where the sum of the distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant.
*   Alternatively, it's the locus of a point where the ratio of its distance from a fixed point (focus) to its distance from a fixed line (directrix) is a constant eccentricity (e), with 0 < e < 1.
*   Ellipses have two perpendicular axes: a major axis (the longest chord) and a minor axis (the shortest chord), intersecting at the center.

## What is an Ellipse?

In simple terms, an ellipse is a closed, two-dimensional curved shape. Imagine stretching a circle; that's an ellipse. It's characterized by two special points inside it, called foci (singular: focus). For any point on the ellipse, if you measure its distance to each focus and add them up, the total will always be the same constant value.

Another way to define an ellipse involves a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix). For any point on the ellipse, the ratio of its distance to the focus to its distance to the directrix is a constant value, known as the eccentricity, 'e'. For an ellipse, this eccentricity always falls between 0 and 1 (0 < e < 1). When the eccentricity is exactly 0, the ellipse becomes a perfect circle.

Ellipses are not just theoretical shapes; they appear in real-world applications such as the design of race tracks, architectural elements, reflective mirrors, and most notably, the celestial orbits of planets around the sun.

## Major and Minor Axes

Every ellipse has two primary axes that define its shape and orientation:

*   **Major Axis:** This is the longest chord of the ellipse. It passes through both foci and the center of the ellipse. The points where the major axis intersects the ellipse are called the vertices.
*   **Minor Axis:** This is the shortest chord of the ellipse. It is perpendicular to the major axis and bisects it at the center of the ellipse. The points where the minor axis intersects the ellipse are often referred to as co-vertices.

The intersection point of the major and minor axes is the **center** of the ellipse. The ellipse is symmetrical about both its major and minor axes.

## Standard Equation of an Ellipse

The standard equation helps us describe an ellipse mathematically. For an ellipse centered at the origin (0,0):

If the major axis lies along the x-axis, the equation is:

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Here, 'a' represents half the length of the major axis, and 'b' represents half the length of the minor axis. In this case, `a > b`.

If the major axis lies along the y-axis, the equation is:

$$\frac{x^2}{b^2} + \frac{y^2}{a^2} = 1$$

Again, 'a' is half the major axis length and 'b' is half the minor axis length, with `a > b`.

The relationship between 'a', 'b', and the eccentricity 'e' is given by: `b² = a²(1 - e²)`, or `c² = a² - b²`, where 'c' is the distance from the center to each focus (`c = ae`).

## Properties of an Ellipse

Here are some key characteristics of an ellipse:

*   It possesses two focal points (foci).
*   It has an associated directrix (a fixed line).
*   Its eccentricity (e) always falls between 0 and 1 (0 < e < 1).
*   The sum of the distances from any point on the ellipse to its two foci is always constant.
*   It has a distinct major axis, minor axis, and a central point.
