# Parabola - General Equations, Properties and Practice Problems

Parabolas are fascinating curves with many real-world applications, from satellite dishes to bridge designs. Understanding parabolas is a fundamental part of coordinate geometry, a crucial topic in Class 11 Mathematics for both board exams and competitive entrance tests like JEE Main.

## Key takeaways

*   A parabola is defined as the path of a point that is always the same distance from a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix).
*   Its eccentricity (e) is exactly 1, making it a distinct type of conic section.
*   The standard equation for a parabola with its axis along the x-axis is y² = 4ax, and along the y-axis is x² = 4ay.
*   This topic is significant for exams; for instance, NTA data shows 18 questions on parabolas in JEE Main between 2013 and 2023, including multiple questions each year.

## What is a Parabola?

Imagine a point moving on a flat surface. If this point always stays an equal distance from a specific fixed point (called the focus) and a particular fixed line (called the directrix), the path it traces is a parabola. This unique property makes the parabola a special type of conic section, specifically one where the eccentricity (e) is equal to 1.

Mathematically, for any point P on the parabola, if S is the focus and M is the foot of the perpendicular from P to the directrix, then PS = PM.

## Standard Equations of a Parabola

The form of a parabola's equation depends on its orientation. The two most common standard forms are:

*   **y² = 4ax**: This represents a parabola that opens to the right, with its axis along the positive x-axis. Here, 'a' is a positive constant.
*   **x² = 4ay**: This represents a parabola that opens upwards, with its axis along the positive y-axis. Again, 'a' is a positive constant.

These equations can be adapted for parabolas opening to the left or downwards by changing the sign of 'a'.

## Derivation of the Standard Equation

Let's derive the equation y² = 4ax. Consider a focus S at (a, 0) and a directrix line x + a = 0 (or x = -a). Let P(x, y) be any point on the parabola.

According to the definition, the distance from P to S (PS) must be equal to the perpendicular distance from P to the directrix (PM).

1.  **Distance PS:** Using the distance formula, PS = √[(x - a)² + (y - 0)²]
2.  **Distance PM:** The perpendicular distance from P(x, y) to the line x + a = 0 is |x + a| / √(1² + 0²) = |x + a|.

Since PS = PM, we can square both sides to remove the square root and absolute value:

PS² = PM²
(x - a)² + y² = (x + a)²
x² - 2ax + a² + y² = x² + 2ax + a²

Subtracting x² + a² from both sides gives:

-2ax + y² = 2ax
y² = 4ax

This is the standard equation for a parabola opening to the right.

## Important Terms Related to a Parabola

Understanding these terms is key to working with parabolas:

*   **Axis**: The line passing through the focus and perpendicular to the directrix. It is the line of symmetry for the parabola. For y² = 4ax, the x-axis is the axis.
*   **Vertex**: The point where the parabola intersects its axis. It is the midpoint between the focus and the directrix. For y² = 4ax, the origin (0,0) is the vertex.
*   **Focal Chord**: Any chord of the parabola that passes through the focus.
*   **Latus Rectum**: A special focal chord that is perpendicular to the axis of the parabola. Its endpoints lie on the parabola. For y² = 4ax, its length is 4a.
*   **Focal Distance**: The distance from the focus to any point on the parabola. By definition, this is equal to the distance from that point to the directrix. For a point P(x, y) on y² = 4ax, the focal distance SP = x + a.

## Other Forms of Parabola

Besides y² = 4ax and x² = 4ay, parabolas can have their vertex at a point (h, k) other than the origin. The general forms are:

*   **(y - k)² = 4a(x - h)**: Parabola with vertex (h, k) opening horizontally.
*   **(x - h)² = 4a(y - k)**: Parabola with vertex (h, k) opening vertically.

These forms are crucial for solving problems where the parabola is shifted from the origin.
