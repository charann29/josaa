# Question: Understanding Pronoun Agreement

Navigating the nuances of English grammar is essential for strong communication, whether you're writing essays for university applications or preparing for competitive exams. This guide focuses on a common grammar point: pronoun agreement, specifically when using the indefinite pronoun "one."

## Key Takeaways

*   When a sentence begins with the indefinite pronoun "one," all subsequent pronouns referring to "one" must also use forms of "one" (e.g., "one's").
*   Avoid switching to other pronouns like "he," "she," "his," or "her" when the subject is "one."
*   Consistent pronoun usage ensures clarity and grammatical correctness.
*   Mastering such grammar rules is crucial for excelling in language proficiency sections of various entrance tests.

## The Indefinite Pronoun "One"

The word "one" can function as an indefinite pronoun, meaning it refers to a person in general rather than a specific individual. When used this way, it sets a grammatical standard for how you refer back to that general person throughout the sentence. Think of it as establishing a consistent voice or perspective. If you start with "one," you need to stick with "one" (or its possessive form, "one's") for continuity.

## Identifying the Error

Let's consider the example sentence: "One (1) / should keep (2) / his word. (3) / No error (4)"

Here, the initial subject is "One." However, in the third part of the sentence, the possessive pronoun switches to "his." This creates a grammatical inconsistency. While "his" would be correct if the subject were "he" or a masculine noun, it's incorrect when the subject is the indefinite pronoun "one."

## The Rule of Consistency

The fundamental rule here is consistency in pronoun agreement. If your sentence begins with "one" as the subject, then any possessive pronoun referring back to that subject must be "one's." This ensures that the grammatical number and person remain uniform throughout the sentence. Changing the pronoun unnecessarily can confuse the reader and is considered a grammatical error.

## Correcting the Sentence

To correct the sentence "One should keep his word," we need to replace "his" with the appropriate possessive form of "one." The correct possessive form is "one's." Therefore, the grammatically correct sentence becomes: "One should keep one's word."

This principle is often tested in grammar sections of exams like the Common Admission Test (CAT) or other verbal ability assessments. Understanding and applying this rule can significantly improve your scores.

Mastering these grammatical nuances will undoubtedly strengthen your communication skills, a valuable asset in any academic or professional journey.
