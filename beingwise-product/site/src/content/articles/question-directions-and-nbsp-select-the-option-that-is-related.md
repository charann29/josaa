# Question: Directions: Select the option that is related to the third word in the same way as the second word is related to the first word. (The words must be considered meaningful English words and must not be related to each other based on the number of letters/consonants/vowels in the word...)

This type of question is a common feature in many competitive exams and aptitude tests in India. It tests your ability to identify relationships between words, which is a key skill for logical reasoning and verbal ability sections. Understanding how these questions work can significantly boost your scores.

## Key Takeaways

*   These questions require you to find a consistent logical connection between word pairs.
*   Focus on the core function, characteristic, or category that links the first two words.
*   Apply the *exact same* relationship to find the missing word for the third term.
*   Always consider the primary, most direct relationship before exploring secondary ones.

## Understanding Analogies

Analogy questions present you with two words that share a specific relationship, often written in the format "A:B :: C:D". Your task is to figure out the relationship between A and B, and then apply that same relationship to C to find D. The colon (:) means "is to," and the double colon (::) means "as."

For example, in "Skin: Touch :: Nose: ?", you need to determine how "Skin" relates to "Touch." Once you identify that, you apply the same logic to "Nose" to find its related word.

## Identifying Relationships

The most crucial step is to correctly identify the relationship between the first pair of words. Common types of relationships include:

*   **Part to Whole:** Finger:Hand
*   **Cause and Effect:** Rain:Flood
*   **Tool and User:** Pen:Writer
*   **Object and Function:** Knife:Cut
*   **Synonyms/Antonyms:** Happy:Joyful / Hot:Cold
*   **Worker and Workplace:** Doctor:Hospital
*   **Category and Item:** Fruit:Apple
*   **Organ and Function:** This is the relationship highlighted in the example question.

In our example, "Skin: Touch," the relationship is that the skin is a sense organ responsible for the sense of touch.

## Applying the Relationship

Once you've nailed down the relationship, apply it directly to the third word. If "Skin" is the organ for "Touch," then "Nose" must be the organ for its primary sense. Let's look at the options provided:

*   **Smell:** The nose is the primary organ for the sense of smell. This fits the pattern perfectly.
*   **Taste:** While smell can influence taste, the tongue is the primary organ for taste. This is not a direct match.
*   **Nose ring:** This is an object *associated* with the nose, but not its primary biological function or sense. The question specifically asks for meaningful English words and not superficial connections.
*   **Sweat:** The nose has sweat glands, but sweating is not its defining sensory function.

Given these choices, "Smell" is the only option that maintains the same "organ:sense" relationship established by "Skin: Touch."

## Why This Matters for Exams

These questions are designed to test not just your vocabulary, but your analytical and logical reasoning skills. They appear in various forms in competitive exams like the UPSC Civil Services Aptitude Test (CSAT), SSC CGL, banking exams (IBPS PO/Clerk), and many university entrance tests. Practicing these helps you think systematically and quickly identify patterns under timed conditions.

Mastering these simple analogies can build a strong foundation for more complex reasoning problems in your academic and professional journey.
