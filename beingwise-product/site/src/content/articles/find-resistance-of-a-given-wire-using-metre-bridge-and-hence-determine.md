# To Find Resistance Of A Given Wire Using Metre Bridge And Hence Determine The Specific Resistance

The metre bridge, often called a slide-wire bridge, is a fundamental instrument in physics laboratories. It's used to accurately measure an unknown electrical resistance and then calculate the specific resistance (resistivity) of the material from which a wire is made.

## Key takeaways

*   The metre bridge operates on the principle of the Wheatstone bridge, allowing for precise resistance measurement.
*   The experiment determines both the unknown resistance of a wire and its material's specific resistance.
*   Accurate measurement of the wire's length and diameter is crucial for calculating specific resistance.
*   Understanding resistance is vital for designing safe and efficient electrical circuits in various applications.

## Aim of the Metre Bridge Experiment

The primary goal of this experiment is to determine the unknown resistance of a given wire using a metre bridge. Following this, we will calculate the specific resistance (resistivity) of the material composing that wire.

## Apparatus Required for Metre Bridge Experiment

To conduct this experiment, you will need the following equipment:

*   A metre bridge
*   A power source (e.g., a Leclanché cell or battery eliminator)
*   A galvanometer
*   A resistance box
*   A jockey
*   A one-way key
*   The resistance wire whose properties are to be determined
*   A screw gauge (for measuring wire diameter)
*   A metre scale
*   A set square
*   Connecting wires
*   Sandpaper (for cleaning connections)

## Theory of Metre Bridge Experiment

(i) The unknown resistance, typically denoted as X, is calculated using the formula derived from the Wheatstone bridge principle:

$X=\frac{(100-l)}{l} \cdot R$

Here, R represents the known resistance taken from the resistance box, placed in the left gap of the metre bridge. The unknown resistance X is connected in the right gap. The variable 'l' (in cm) is the length of the metre bridge wire from the zero end up to the balance point (null point).

(ii) The specific resistance (resistivity), denoted by $\rho$, of the material of the given wire is then determined using the formula:

$\rho=\frac{X \pi D^2}{4 L}$

In this equation, L is the total length of the resistance wire used in the experiment, and D is its diameter.

## Procedure for Metre Bridge Experiment

Follow these steps to perform the experiment and find the resistance:

1.  **Setup Assembly:** Arrange all the apparatus as per the standard circuit diagram for a metre bridge.
2.  **Wire Connection:** Connect the resistance wire, whose resistance you aim to find, into the right gap of the metre bridge (between terminals C and B). Ensure no part of the wire is looped.
3.  **Resistance Box Connection:** Place a low-range resistance box in the left gap of the metre bridge (between terminals A and B).
4.  **Complete Connections:** Make all other necessary electrical connections as shown in the circuit diagram, ensuring they are firm and clean.
5.  **Initial Resistance:** Take out a suitable resistance (e.g., 2 ohms) from the resistance box and insert the one-way key K to close the circuit.
6.  **Connection Check:** Gently touch the jockey first to the left end and then to the right end of the metre bridge wire. Observe the galvanometer. If it shows deflections in opposite directions, your connections are correct. If deflections are only in one direction, there's a fault in the circuit that needs to be rectified before proceeding.
7.  **Finding the Null Point:** Carefully slide the jockey along the metre bridge wire from left to right. Look for the point where the galvanometer shows zero deflection. This position is the null point, labelled D.
8.  **Record Balance Length:** Note down the length 'l' from the zero end of the wire to this null point D. This is your balance length.
9.  **Repeat and Vary:** Change the resistance taken from the resistance box and repeat steps 6-8 to obtain several readings. This helps in minimizing experimental errors.
10. **Measure Wire Dimensions:** Once resistance measurements are complete, carefully measure the total length (L) of the resistance wire using a metre scale and its diameter (D) using a screw gauge. Take multiple diameter readings along the wire and calculate the average.

This experiment provides a practical understanding of electrical resistance and its precise measurement, skills that are fundamental in various engineering and technological fields.
