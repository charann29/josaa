# Electron Affinity - Introduction, Definition, Trends, FAQs

Atoms behave differently when it comes to gaining electrons; some readily accept them, while others put up a strong resistance. This tendency of an atom to acquire an electron and form a stable negative ion is explained by electron affinity, which describes the energy change involved when an electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom.

## Key Takeaways

*   Electron affinity (EA) measures the energy released when a neutral gaseous atom gains an electron.
*   A more negative EA value indicates a greater tendency for an atom to accept an electron.
*   EA is a periodic property influenced by factors like atomic size and nuclear charge.
*   Halogens exhibit high electron affinity due to their need for just one more electron to complete their outer shell.

## What is Electron Affinity (EA)?

Electron affinity is defined as the energy change that occurs when an electron is added to a neutral, isolated gaseous atom to form a negative ion. Essentially, it quantifies how eager an atom is to accept an additional electron. This value is typically expressed in kilojoules per mole (kJ/mol).

A higher (more negative) electron affinity value means that more energy is released when an electron is gained, indicating a stronger attraction for the incoming electron and a greater likelihood of forming a stable negative ion. Conversely, a less negative or positive value suggests less attraction or even resistance to gaining an electron.

The process can be represented as:

`X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g) + Energy`

## First and Second Electron Affinity

When we talk about electron affinity, it's important to distinguish between the first and subsequent electron additions:

*   **First Electron Affinity:** This refers to the energy change when the first electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom. This process is usually exothermic, meaning energy is released, resulting in a negative EA value. This reflects the atom's natural attraction for an electron to achieve a more stable configuration.
*   **Second Electron Affinity:** This describes the energy change when a second electron is added to an already negatively charged ion. This process is always endothermic, meaning energy must be supplied to force the electron onto an already negative ion. The existing negative charge repels the incoming electron, requiring external energy to overcome this repulsion.

## Electron Affinity of Halogens

Halogens, found in Group 17 of the periodic table, include elements like fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), iodine (I), and astatine (At). These non-metallic elements are highly reactive because they possess seven valence electrons and only need one more to achieve a stable octet configuration, similar to noble gases. This strong desire to complete their outer shell makes them excellent electron acceptors.

While electron affinity generally decreases as you move down a group due to increasing atomic size and reduced nuclear attraction for an incoming electron, there's an interesting exception with fluorine and chlorine. Despite fluorine being above chlorine in the group, chlorine actually has a higher (more negative) electron affinity than fluorine. This is attributed to fluorine's very small atomic size, which leads to greater electron-electron repulsion within its compact electron shell, making it slightly less favorable to accept an additional electron compared to the larger chlorine atom.

## Electron Affinity Trends

Understanding electron affinity helps predict an element's chemical behavior. Here are the general trends:

*   **Across a Period (Left to Right):** As you move from left to right across a period in the periodic table, electron affinity generally increases (becomes more negative). This is because the nuclear charge increases, pulling the valence electrons more strongly and making it easier for the atom to attract an additional electron into its outer shell.
*   **Down a Group (Top to Bottom):** As you move down a group, electron affinity generally decreases (becomes less negative). This is due to the increasing atomic size and the addition of new electron shells. The outermost electrons, and any incoming electron, are further from the nucleus and experience less attraction, leading to a smaller energy release upon electron gain.

However, these are general trends, and exceptions can occur due to factors like electron-electron repulsion in very small atoms (as seen with fluorine) or the stability of half-filled or fully-filled subshells.

Understanding electron affinity is crucial for grasping the reactivity of elements and how they form chemical bonds, offering valuable insights into the fundamental properties described in official resources like NCERT textbooks for Class 11 Chemistry.
