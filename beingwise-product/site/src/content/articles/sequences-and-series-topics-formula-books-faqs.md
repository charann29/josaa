# Sequences and Series - Topics, Formula, Books, FAQs

Mathematics often reveals hidden patterns in the world around us, from the growth of plants to financial planning. Sequences and series are fundamental concepts that help us understand and describe these ordered patterns numerically.

## Key Takeaways

*   A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers following a specific rule, while a **series** is the sum of the terms in a sequence.
*   Key types include Arithmetic Progressions (AP) and Geometric Progressions (GP), each with distinct formulas for terms and sums.
*   Mastering these concepts is crucial for Class 11 exams and competitive entrance tests like JEE Main.
*   Real-world applications range from calculating interest to modeling population growth and scientific phenomena.

## Understanding Sequences and Series

A sequence is essentially a list of numbers arranged in a particular order, where each number (or 'term') is related to the previous one by a definite rule. For instance, the sequence 2, 4, 6, 8... follows the rule of adding 2 to the previous term. When you add up the terms of a sequence, you get a series.

### Types of Sequences

Sequences can be categorized in a couple of ways:

*   **Finite Sequences:** These have a limited, countable number of terms. For example, 2, 4, 6, 8 is a finite sequence with four terms.
*   **Infinite Sequences:** These continue without end, often indicated by an ellipsis (...). For example, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10... is an infinite sequence.

Beyond their length, sequences are also classified by the relationship between their terms:

*   **Arithmetic Progression (AP):** In an AP, the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is called the 'common difference'.
    *   **nth term formula:** `a_n = a + (n-1)d` (where `a` is the first term, `d` is the common difference, and `n` is the term number)
    *   **Sum of n terms formula:** `S_n = n/2 [2a + (n-1)d]` or `S_n = n/2 (a + l)` (where `l` is the last term)
*   **Geometric Progression (GP):** In a GP, the ratio between consecutive terms is constant. This constant ratio is called the 'common ratio'.
    *   **nth term formula:** `a_n = ar^(n-1)` (where `a` is the first term, `r` is the common ratio, and `n` is the term number)
    *   **Sum of n terms formula:** `S_n = a(r^n - 1) / (r - 1)` (for `r â  1`)
