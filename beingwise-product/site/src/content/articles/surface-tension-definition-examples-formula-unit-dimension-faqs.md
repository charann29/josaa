# Surface Tension - Definition, Examples, Formula, Unit, Dimension, FAQs

Surface tension is a fascinating property of liquids that explains why their surfaces often behave like a thin, stretched elastic film. It's the reason you see water form neat, rounded droplets or why certain insects can effortlessly glide across a pond.

## Key Takeaways

*   Surface tension is the force per unit length acting on a liquid's surface, causing it to minimize its surface area.
*   It arises from the unbalanced attractive forces between molecules at the liquid's surface compared to those within the bulk liquid.
*   The SI unit for surface tension is Newtons per meter (N/m), and its dimensional formula is MT⁻².
*   Surface tension decreases as the temperature of the liquid increases.

## What is Surface Tension?

Surface tension describes the tendency of a liquid's surface to contract to the smallest possible area. Imagine drawing an invisible line on the surface of a liquid; surface tension is the force that acts perpendicularly to this line, tangential to the surface, for every unit of its length.

This force is specific to the liquid itself and doesn't depend on the size of the surface area being considered or the length of the imaginary line. It is a scalar quantity, meaning it has magnitude but no direction.

### Formula for Surface Tension

The mathematical representation of surface tension (T) is given by:

$T = \frac{F}{l}$

Where:

*   **F** represents the force acting on the liquid's surface.
*   **l** is the length over which this force acts.

### Unit and Dimensional Formula

*   **SI Unit:** Since force is measured in Newtons (N) and length in meters (m), the SI unit for surface tension is Newtons per meter (N/m).
*   **Dimensional Formula:** The dimensional formula for surface tension is MT⁻².

## What Causes Surface Tension?

Surface tension is fundamentally caused by the intermolecular forces of attraction between liquid molecules. Deep within a liquid, each molecule is surrounded by others, experiencing balanced attractive forces from all directions, resulting in zero net force.

However, molecules located at the very surface of the liquid are in a different situation. They lack neighboring molecules above them. Consequently, they experience a net inward pull from the molecules below and beside them. This unbalanced inward force causes the surface molecules to be pulled closer together, making the liquid's surface contract and behave much like a stretched elastic film. This tendency to minimize surface area is what we observe as surface tension.

## Everyday Examples of Surface Tension

Surface tension is responsible for many common phenomena we encounter:

*   **Spherical Raindrops:** Raindrops and small water droplets naturally form spheres because, for a given volume, a sphere has the smallest possible surface area. Surface tension works to achieve this minimum area.
*   **Insects Walking on Water:** Small insects, such as water striders, can walk on the surface of water without sinking. Their light weight is supported by the thin, elastic-like film created by the water's surface tension.
*   **Floating Needle:** If carefully placed, a small metallic needle or a razor blade can float on water, even though its density is greater than water. Surface tension provides the necessary upward force.
*   **Soap Bubbles:** Soap solutions readily form bubbles. Surface tension pulls the liquid into a thin, spherical film, creating the bubble's structure.
*   **Formation of Drops from a Tap:** When water drips from a tap, it doesn't immediately break off. Instead, it collects and forms a rounded drop, held together by surface tension, before finally detaching.

## Temperature Dependence

An important characteristic of surface tension is its relationship with temperature. As the temperature of a liquid increases, its surface tension generally decreases. This happens because higher temperatures mean molecules have more kinetic energy, which weakens the intermolecular forces of attraction, thus reducing the inward pull at the surface.
