# Higher Education Institutes (HEIs) in India Lack Gender Diversity

Gender diversity in leadership roles within India's higher education sector is a critical issue that demands attention. While significant progress is being made in various areas of education, the representation of women at the helm of universities and institutions remains remarkably low.

## Key takeaways

*   Only about 11.24% of Higher Education Institutes (HEIs) in India are led by women.
*   This figure, though slightly improved from 9.56% in 2021, still highlights a significant gender gap compared to global standards.
*   Government-funded institutions show better female representation in leadership than private universities and Institutes of National Importance.
*   Technical education, including top IITs, shows a near-complete absence of women in leadership positions.

## The Alarming Gender Gap in Leadership

A recent analysis of 1352 Higher Education Institutes across India, encompassing IITs, IIMs, public, private, and deemed universities, revealed a concerning trend. Out of these institutions, only 152 are currently headed by women. This translates to an incredibly low ratio of 11.24%.

This statistic points to a deep-seated bias and structural flaw within our society that continues to limit women's progression into top leadership roles in education. While there has been a modest increase of approximately two percentage points from a similar study in 2021 (which reported 9.56%), the current figure of 11.24% still leaves much room for improvement.

## Global Comparisons: How India Stacks Up

When we look at the global landscape, India's figures for women in HEI leadership positions lag significantly behind many developed nations. For instance, countries like the US, UK, and Australia report that around 25% of their higher education institutions are led by women. Brazil stands out with an even higher ratio of 47.3%.

Globally, approximately 25% of the world's top 200 universities have women as their leaders. This comparison underscores the urgent need for structural reforms within India's education system to foster greater gender diversity and to celebrate the achievements of successful women in academia, inspiring future generations.

## Bright Spots in Government Institutions

Despite the overall low numbers, there's a glimmer of hope in the performance of government-funded institutions. Central Universities lead the way with 14.04% female leaders. Deemed universities follow closely at 13.76%, and state public universities are at 13.2%.

In contrast, private universities show an average performance with 9.18% female leaders. Institutes of National Importance (INIs), which include many of India's premier technical institutions, unfortunately, are at the bottom with only 8.53% female representation in leadership.

## The Technical Education Gap

Perhaps the most concerning finding is the near-complete absence of women in leadership within the technical education sector. Not a single IIT (Indian Institute of Technology) is currently headed by a woman. The situation is only slightly better in premier management institutes, with just 2 out of 21 IIMs (Indian Institutes of Management) having female leaders.

This disparity extends to other technical institutions as well. Out of 120 IITs, IIITs, NITs, and IISERs combined, only two institutions (one NIT and one IIIT) are led by women. This stark reality is often attributed to the lower enrollment of women in technical education streams, creating a smaller pool of potential female leaders for these institutes.

## Steps to Improve Gender Diversity in HEIs

Addressing the gender imbalance in HEI leadership requires a comprehensive, multi-faceted approach. This involves a combination of supportive policy measures and broader socio-economic reforms aimed at actively promoting and nurturing diversity in top leadership roles. It's time for all stakeholders to collaborate and make decisive efforts to ensure that gender diversity in India's HEIs reaches a truly respectable and equitable level.
