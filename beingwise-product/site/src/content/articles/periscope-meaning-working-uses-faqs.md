A periscope is a clever optical tool that lets you see things that are otherwise hidden from your direct view, often because they are at a different height. It works on the basic principle of light reflection, making it possible to observe surroundings from a concealed or lower position.

## Key takeaways

*   A periscope uses two plane mirrors positioned at a 45-degree angle within a tube.
*   It operates by reflecting light from an object to an observer, even if there's an obstruction.
*   Invented by Hippolyte Marie-Davy in 1854, periscopes have significant historical and practical uses.
*   Common applications include submarines for underwater observation and trenches for military surveillance.

## What is a Periscope?

Imagine needing to see over a wall or around a corner without exposing yourself. That's exactly what a periscope helps you do. At its core, a periscope is a tube equipped with two mirrors. These mirrors, typically flat (plane mirrors), are crucial to its function. They are arranged in such a way that light from an object enters the periscope at one end, bounces off the first mirror, then off the second mirror, and finally reaches the observer's eye at the other end. This setup allows you to 'peek' into areas that are not directly visible.

## How a Periscope is Constructed

The construction of a simple periscope is quite straightforward. It generally consists of a long tube, which might have bends, and two plane mirrors. These mirrors are positioned near the two openings of the tube, each fixed at an angle of 45 degrees relative to the tube's length. They face each other, but are offset, ensuring that light entering the top mirror is directed downwards to the bottom mirror, and then outwards to the observer's eye. The exact angle is key to ensuring the light travels efficiently from the object to the observer, allowing for clear vision.

## How a Periscope Works

The working principle of a periscope is based on simple reflection. When light rays from an object hit the first mirror (the upper one) inside the periscope, they are reflected downwards. These reflected rays then travel to the second mirror (the lower one). The second mirror, also positioned at a 45-degree angle, reflects these light rays horizontally towards the observer's eye. Essentially, the first mirror creates an image that acts as the 'object' for the second mirror, which then produces the final image that the observer sees. This series of reflections allows the observer to view objects that are not in their direct line of sight.

## Uses of a Periscope

Periscopes have been incredibly useful in various fields, especially where observation from a hidden or protected position is necessary:

*   **Submarines:** One of the most famous uses is in submarines. A periscope allows the crew to observe the surface of the water and surrounding vessels or land without surfacing the entire submarine, maintaining stealth and safety.
*   **Trenches:** Historically, during wartime, soldiers in trenches used periscopes to observe enemy movements without exposing themselves to direct fire.
*   **Vehicles:** Some armored vehicles and tanks also incorporate periscopes to provide drivers and commanders with a wider field of vision from inside the protected cabin.
*   **Educational Tools:** Simple periscopes are often built in schools as a hands-on way to teach principles of light and reflection.

Periscopes, with their simple yet effective design, continue to be a testament to the power of basic optical principles in solving practical observation challenges.
