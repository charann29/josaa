# Graphical Representation of Quadratic Equation

Understanding the graphical representation of quadratic equations is a fundamental concept in mathematics. These graphs, known as parabolas, are crucial for visualising solutions and understanding the behaviour of quadratic functions, a key topic in Class 11 Mathematics for both board exams and competitive entrance tests like JEE Main, SRMJEE, BITSAT, WBJEE, and BCECE.

## Key Takeaways

*   A quadratic equation's graph is always a U-shaped curve called a parabola.
*   The direction a parabola opens (upwards or downwards) is determined by the sign of the 'a' coefficient in the equation.
*   The vertex represents the highest or lowest point of the parabola, indicating maximum or minimum values.
*   The axis of symmetry is a vertical line that perfectly divides the parabola into two mirror images.

## What is a Parabola Graph?

A parabola is the distinctive U-shaped curve that results from plotting a quadratic function, typically written in the standard form `f(x) = ax² + bx + c`. The orientation of this 'U' shape depends entirely on the value of 'a':

*   If `a > 0` (a is positive), the parabola opens upwards.
*   If `a < 0` (a is negative), the parabola opens downwards.

This simple rule dictates the direction and overall appearance of your quadratic graph.

### Vertex

The vertex is the most extreme point on a parabola. If the parabola opens upwards, the vertex is the lowest point, representing the minimum value of the function. If it opens downwards, the vertex is the highest point, representing the maximum value.

### Axis of Symmetry

This is a vertical line that passes directly through the vertex of the parabola. It acts like a mirror, ensuring that one side of the parabola is an exact reflection of the other. For a quadratic equation in standard form, `f(x) = ax² + bx + c`, the equation for the axis of symmetry is `x = -b / (2a)`.

### Y-intercept

The y-intercept is the point where the parabola crosses the y-axis. Every parabola will cross the y-axis at exactly one point. In the standard form `f(x) = ax² + bx + c`, the y-intercept is simply `(0, c)`.

### X-intercepts

The x-intercepts are the points where the parabola crosses the x-axis. These points are especially significant because they represent the roots (or solutions) of the quadratic equation, where `f(x) = 0`. A parabola can have two, one (if the vertex is on the x-axis), or no x-intercepts (if it never crosses the x-axis).

## Standard Form of Parabola Equation

The most common way to write a quadratic equation is the standard form:

`f(x) = y = ax² + bx + c`

As discussed, the sign of 'a' determines the parabola's orientation:

*   `a > 0`: Parabola opens upwards.
*   `a < 0`: Parabola opens downwards.

From this form, the axis of symmetry can be found using the formula: `x = -b / (2a)`.

## Vertex Form of Parabola Equation

Another useful way to express a quadratic equation is the vertex form, which directly highlights the coordinates of the vertex:

`f(x) = y = a(x - h)² + k`

In this form, `(h, k)` directly represents the coordinates of the parabola's vertex. Similar to the standard form, the value of 'a' still dictates the parabola's direction:

*   If `a > 0`, the parabola opens upwards.
*   If `a < 0`, the parabola opens downwards.

## How to Graph a Quadratic Function

To graph a quadratic function `y = f(x) = ax² + bx + c` (where `a, b, c` are real numbers and `a ≠ 0`), you can follow these steps:

1.  **Determine Direction:** Look at the sign of 'a'. If `a > 0`, it opens up; if `a < 0`, it opens down.
2.  **Find the Vertex:** Calculate the x-coordinate of the vertex using `x = -b / (2a)`. Substitute this x-value back into the original equation to find the corresponding y-coordinate. This gives you `(h, k)`.
3.  **Find the Y-intercept:** Set `x = 0` in the equation to find the y-intercept `(0, c)`.
4.  **Find X-intercepts (Roots):** Set `y = 0` and solve the quadratic equation `ax² + bx + c = 0` for x. You can use the quadratic formula `x = [-b ± sqrt(b² - 4ac)] / (2a)`. These are your x-intercepts.
5.  **Plot Points and Sketch:** Plot the vertex, intercepts, and a few additional points if needed (using the symmetry around the axis of symmetry helps). Then, draw a smooth U-shaped curve connecting these points.

Understanding these graphical elements provides a powerful way to visualise and solve quadratic equations.
