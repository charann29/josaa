# Classic British Aircraft: The English Electric Canberra

The English Electric Canberra holds a special place in aviation history as a remarkable British-designed jet bomber. This page explores a fascinating journey simulating the flight of this iconic aircraft around the world.

## Key Takeaways

*   The simulated journey began and concluded at RAF Wittering in England, highlighting its British origins.
*   The route covered extensive stretches across Europe, Asia, and North America, showcasing the Canberra's potential global reach.
*   The simulation included challenging segments, such as 'Flying the Hump' over the Himalayas and crossing the vast Atlantic Ocean.
*   The detailed flight log offers a unique perspective on long-distance navigation and potential challenges faced by pilots.

## The European Leg: From England to the East

The journey commenced with a series of domestic flights within England, starting and ending at RAF Wittering, with stops at Marham, Manston, and Boscombe Down. Following this, the Canberra simulated a cross-channel flight to Cherbourg, France, before heading to Guernsey in the UK Channel Islands. From there, the route continued through Europe, with segments to Marseilles, Venice, Zagreb, Belgrade, and finally, Istanbul, marking a significant progression eastward.

## Across Asia: A Grand Expedition

After Istanbul, the simulated flight ventured deeper into Asia. Key stops included Cyprus, Beirut, and a multi-part journey to Baghdad and Tehran. The path then led through Gorgan and Ashgabat, where a segment to Sarakhs involved a simulated 'getting lost' scenario, adding a layer of realism to the navigation challenges. Further Asian destinations included Termez, Kabul, Islamabad, Lahore, and a two-part flight to Delhi. The journey then turned towards the Himalayas, with a two-part flight to Kathmandu, followed by a multi-part segment to Lokpriya Gopinath Bordoloi International Airport in Guwahati.

## Navigating the Hump and China

One of the most historically significant and challenging parts of the route was 'Flying the Hump' – the perilous air route over the Himalayas between India and China. This segment was simulated in two parts. Once over China, the journey continued with multiple flights between various Chinese cities, including ZUUU, ZLXY, ZBNY, ZBTJ, and ZYTL, demonstrating extensive travel within the region.

## The Pacific Crossing and North America

From China, the Canberra's simulated route extended to South Korea (RKSI) and then, notably, to North Korea (ZKWS). The journey then took a significant leap across the Pacific to Russia, with stops at UHWW, UHSI, and UHPP, before reaching the USA at PASY (Attu Island). The North American leg involved numerous flights across Alaska, with destinations like PADK, PAAK, PADU, PACD, and PADQ (Kodiak), culminating at PANC (Anchorage International). From Alaska, the route continued into Canada, encompassing flights to Burwash, Watson Lake, Fort Nelson, and a series of other Canadian airports, including several diversions due to simulated bad weather, highlighting real-world operational considerations.

## The Atlantic Return and Final Leg

After extensive travel across Canada, the simulated journey proceeded to Prince Edward Island (CYSU) and then to Gander International (CYQX) in Newfoundland, preparing for the monumental Atlantic crossing. This trans-Atlantic flight was simulated in several parts, reflecting the immense distance and endurance required. The final leg of the journey brought the Canberra back to Europe, landing at Stornoway in Scotland, before completing its epic global circumnavigation with a final two-part flight back to RAF Wittering, England, where it all began.

This incredible simulated journey provides a detailed look at the capabilities and operational scope of the English Electric Canberra, tracing a path that mirrors the adventurous spirit of aviation.
