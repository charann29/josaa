Understanding how light behaves when it passes through different materials is fundamental in physics. This experiment helps us determine the refractive index of a glass slab, which tells us how much light bends when it enters the glass. We use a travelling microscope, a highly precise tool, to measure the real and apparent thickness of the glass, allowing us to calculate this important optical property.

## Key takeaways

*   The refractive index quantifies how much light bends when it enters a new medium like glass.
*   A travelling microscope provides accurate measurements of small vertical distances, crucial for this experiment.
*   By comparing the actual thickness of a glass slab to its apparent thickness (how thick it seems through the microscope), we can calculate its refractive index.
*   This method is a reliable and direct way to understand the optical characteristics of transparent materials.

## Aim of the Experiment

The primary goal of this experiment is to accurately determine the refractive index of a given glass slab using a travelling microscope.

## Required Apparatus

To conduct this experiment, you will need:

*   Three glass slabs of varying thicknesses, but made from the same material. A glass slab is a transparent block with parallel, rectangular faces.
*   A travelling microscope.
*   Lycopodium powder.

### Understanding the Travelling Microscope

A travelling microscope is essentially a compound microscope mounted vertically on a precise scale. It can be moved up and down, and it includes a vernier scale that slides along the main vertical scale. Readings are taken by combining the main scale and vernier scale measurements, ensuring high accuracy for small vertical displacements.

## Theoretical Basis

The refractive index (often denoted by the Greek letter 'mu', $\mu$) is defined as the ratio of the real thickness of the slab to its apparent thickness. When you look through a transparent material like glass, objects behind it appear closer than they actually are due to the bending of light. This difference is what we measure:

$$\mu = \frac{\text{Real thickness of the slab}}{\text{Apparent thickness of the slab}}$$

## Experimental Procedure

### Adjusting the Travelling Microscope

1.  Position the travelling microscope (M) on a stable table, ideally near a window to ensure adequate lighting.
2.  Use the levelling screws to ensure the microscope's base is perfectly horizontal.
3.  Adjust the microscope itself to be horizontal. Then, adjust the eyepiece until the cross-wires inside are clearly in focus.
4.  Determine the vernier constant (or least count) of the microscope's vertical scale. This value is critical for accurate measurements.

### Performing the Measurements

5.  Draw a clear cross-mark with black ink directly on the base of the microscope. This mark will serve as point P.
6.  Make sure the microscope is vertical. Focus it precisely on the cross-mark at P, ensuring there is no parallax error between the cross-wires and the image of the mark P.
7.  Record the main scale and vernier scale readings (let's call this R1) from the vertical scale.
8.  Carefully place the glass slab with the least thickness directly over the mark P.
9.  Raise the microscope upwards and refocus it on the image (P1) of the cross-mark, which will now appear elevated due to the glass slab.
10. Note this new reading (R2) on the vertical scale.
11. Sprinkle a few particles of lycopodium powder evenly on the top surface of the glass slab.
12. Raise the microscope further and focus it on a particle of lycopodium powder near the center of the slab's surface (let's call this point S).
13. Record this final reading (R3) on the vertical scale.
14. Repeat steps 8-13 using a different glass slab with greater thickness.
15. Systematically record all your observations in a table.

## Calculations

First, note the vernier constant (least count) for the vertical scale of the microscope. This is the smallest measurement the instrument can accurately distinguish.

The real thickness of the glass slab is the actual physical thickness, measured from the bottom surface (where the cross-mark appears) to the top surface (where the lycopodium powder is). This corresponds to the difference between R3 and R1.

The apparent thickness is how thick the slab appears when viewed through the microscope, which is the difference between the top surface reading (R3) and the apparent position of the cross-mark (R2).

Therefore, the refractive index ($\mu$) is calculated as:

$$\mu = \frac{\text{Real thickness of the slab}}{\text{Apparent thickness of the slab}} = \frac{R_3 - R_1}{R_3 - R_2}$$
