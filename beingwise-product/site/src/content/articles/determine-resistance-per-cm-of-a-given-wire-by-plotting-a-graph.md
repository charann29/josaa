# To Determine Resistance per CM of a Given Wire by Plotting a Graph for Potential Difference versus Current

Understanding how electrical resistance works is fundamental in physics. This experiment helps us precisely determine the resistance per centimetre of a specific wire by observing the relationship between the potential difference (voltage) across it and the current flowing through it, and then plotting this data on a graph.

## Key takeaways

*   Ohm's Law is the foundational principle: Voltage is directly proportional to current in a conductor, given constant physical conditions.
*   Plotting potential difference (V) against current (I) yields a straight line, and the slope of this line represents the total resistance (R) of the wire.
*   By dividing the total resistance by the wire's length, we can calculate its resistance per centimetre.
*   This method offers a clear, practical way to understand how a material's resistance relates to its physical dimensions.

## Aim of the Experiment

The primary goal is to find the resistance per centimetre of a given wire. We achieve this by collecting data on potential difference and current, then using this data to create a graph, from which the total resistance is derived.

## Essential Equipment

To conduct this experiment accurately, you will need:

*   A specific resistance wire
*   A voltmeter (typically with a range of 0-3 V)
*   An ammeter (typically with a range of 0-3 A)
*   A power source, such as a battery or a battery eliminator
*   A rheostat (variable resistor)
*   A meter scale for measuring wire length
*   A one-way key (switch)
*   Connecting wires
*   A piece of sandpaper for cleaning connections

## The Underlying Theory: Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is central to this experiment. It states that for a conductor, the electric current passing through it is directly proportional to the potential difference applied across its ends, provided that physical conditions like temperature, dimensions, and pressure remain constant. Mathematically, if 'I' is the current and 'V' is the potential difference, then:

$V \propto I$

This can be written as:

$V = RI$

Here, 'R' is the constant of proportionality, known as the resistance of the conductor. Therefore, resistance can be calculated as:

$R = \frac{V}{I}$

Resistance 'R' is influenced by the material of the conductor, its temperature, and its physical dimensions. In the International System of Units (SI), potential difference (V) is measured in volts, current (I) in amperes, and resistance (R) in ohms (Ω).

The experiment relies on two key theoretical points:

1.  If Ohm's Law holds, the ratio V/I should be constant for a given resistance. Consequently, a graph plotting potential difference (V) against current (I) should be a straight line passing through the origin.
2.  The constant slope of this straight line directly gives the unknown resistance value (R).

## Step-by-Step Procedure

Careful execution of the following steps is crucial for accurate results:

1.  **Set Up:** Assemble all the apparatus according to the standard circuit diagram for determining resistance. Ensure all components are correctly placed.
2.  **Prepare Connections:** Use sandpaper to clean the ends of all connecting wires. This removes any insulation or oxidation that might hinder electrical contact.
3.  **Make Connections:** Create neat, secure, and tight connections as shown in the circuit diagram. Importantly, ensure that the positive terminals of the voltmeter and ammeter are connected towards the positive terminal of the power source.
4.  **Initial Checks:** Determine the least count of both the voltmeter and ammeter. Also, check for any zero error in either instrument and account for it in your readings.
5.  **Functionality Test:** Insert the key (switch) to close the circuit. Adjust the rheostat's sliding contact and confirm that both the ammeter and voltmeter are functioning correctly.
6.  **Adjust Current:** Adjust the rheostat to allow a measurable amount of current to flow through the resistance wire.
7.  **Record Readings:** Note down the potential difference (V) displayed on the voltmeter and the corresponding current (I) from the ammeter.
8.  **Vary Readings:** Slightly move the rheostat's sliding contact. Aim to get new readings where both the ammeter and voltmeter show readings that are on full divisions, avoiding fractional readings where possible for easier data collection.
9.  **Repeat:** Record several pairs of (V, I) readings by adjusting the rheostat multiple times. For battery eliminators, typically adjust the voltage knob to different settings (e.g., 2V, 4V, 6V) and record readings for each.

## Calculating Resistance and Resistance per Centimetre

Once you have collected several pairs of (V, I) readings, you will plot these on a graph with potential difference (V) on the y-axis and current (I) on the x-axis. Draw the best-fit straight line through these points. The slope of this line will give you the total resistance (R) of the wire.

Measure the exact length of the resistance wire used in the circuit (in centimetres). Finally, divide the total resistance (R) by the measured length of the wire to obtain the resistance per centimetre.

This experiment provides a hands-on understanding of Ohm's Law and its practical application in determining material properties.
