# Hund's Rule - Definition, Examples, Uses, Spin Multiplicity, FAQs

Hund's rule is a foundational concept in chemistry, explaining how electrons arrange themselves within an atom's orbitals to achieve stability and influence its magnetic properties. It helps us understand why electrons prefer to occupy separate spaces before pairing up.

## Key takeaways

*   Hund's rule dictates the filling order of electrons in degenerate (same energy) orbitals.
*   Electrons first occupy each orbital singly with parallel spins before any pairing occurs.
*   This arrangement minimizes electron-electron repulsion, leading to a more stable atomic state.
*   The rule is crucial for predicting an atom's ground state electronic configuration and its magnetic behavior.

## What is Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity?

Friedrich Hund proposed this rule in 1925. It essentially states that when electrons are added to a subshell containing orbitals of equal energy (like the three p orbitals or five d orbitals), they will first fill each orbital individually, all with the same spin direction. Only after all orbitals in that subshell have one electron will any pairing of electrons begin.

This principle is often called the "rule of maximum multiplicity" because this arrangement leads to the maximum possible number of unpaired electrons, and thus the maximum spin multiplicity, which generally corresponds to the lowest energy state for an atom. Minimizing electron-electron repulsion by keeping electrons in separate orbitals with parallel spins makes the atom more stable.

## Understanding Electron Spin and Multiplicity

Electrons possess an intrinsic property called spin angular momentum, which can be thought of as either "spin up" or "spin down." When two electrons occupy the same orbital, they must have opposite spins (Pauli Exclusion Principle). However, in different orbitals, they can have the same spin. Hund's rule prioritizes this parallel spin arrangement.

Spin multiplicity is a value calculated as (2S + 1), where 'S' is the total spin angular momentum of all electrons. For each unpaired electron, S contributes ±1/2. When electrons have parallel spins, their spin contributions add up, leading to a higher 'S' value and thus a higher multiplicity. For instance, two unpaired electrons with parallel spins (both +1/2) give S = 1, and multiplicity = 2(1) + 1 = 3. This maximum multiplicity state is energetically favored.

## Hund's Rule with an Example: Nitrogen Atom

Let's consider the nitrogen atom (atomic number 7) as an example. Its electronic configuration is 1s²2s²2p³.

*   **1s orbital:** Contains two electrons, paired with opposite spins.
*   **2s orbital:** Contains two electrons, paired with opposite spins.
*   **2p orbitals:** There are three 2p orbitals (2px, 2py, 2pz), all having the same energy. According to Hund's rule, the three electrons in the 2p subshell will not pair up. Instead, each of the three 2p orbitals will receive one electron, and all three of these electrons will have parallel spins (e.g., all spin up).

If Hund's rule were not followed, one might incorrectly place two electrons in one 2p orbital and one in another, leaving one 2p orbital empty. This would result in higher electron-electron repulsion and a less stable state.

## Uses of Hund's Rule

Hund's rule is fundamental for several aspects of chemistry and physics:

*   **Predicting Ground State:** It helps determine the most stable (ground state) electronic configuration of atoms and ions, which is crucial for understanding their chemical reactivity.
*   **Explaining Magnetic Properties:** The presence of unpaired electrons, as predicted by Hund's rule, gives rise to paramagnetism (attraction to a magnetic field). Atoms with all paired electrons are diamagnetic (repelled by a magnetic field). For example, oxygen (2p⁴) has two unpaired electrons due to Hund's rule, making it paramagnetic.
*   **Understanding Atomic Spectra:** The rule is based on observations from atomic spectra and helps in interpreting these spectra to deduce electronic structures.
*   **Quantum Chemistry:** It's a cornerstone in more advanced quantum mechanical calculations for molecular orbitals and bonding.

By following Hund's rule, we can accurately depict how electrons occupy orbitals, leading to a deeper understanding of an atom's intrinsic properties.
