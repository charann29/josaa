Understanding how gases behave under different conditions is fundamental in chemistry and physics. The gas laws describe the relationships between a gas's pressure, volume, and temperature, offering crucial insights into their properties.

## Key Takeaways

*   **Boyle's Law** explains the inverse relationship between a gas's pressure and volume when temperature is kept constant.
*   **Charles's Law** demonstrates that a gas's volume is directly proportional to its absolute temperature at constant pressure.
*   **Gay-Lussac's Law** focuses on the direct relationship between pressure and absolute temperature when volume is constant.
*   **The Ideal Gas Law** combines these principles into a single equation, providing a comprehensive model for ideal gas behavior.

## Boyle's Law: Pressure and Volume

Boyle's Law, named after Robert Boyle, states that for a fixed amount of an ideal gas at a constant temperature, the volume it occupies is inversely proportional to the pressure exerted on it. This means if you increase the pressure, the volume will decrease, and vice versa, as long as the temperature doesn't change.

Mathematically, this relationship is expressed as:

$V \propto \frac{1}{P}$

Or, more commonly:

$PV = \text{constant}$

This leads to the useful equation for comparing two states of a gas:

$P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$

Where:
*   $P_1$ and $V_1$ are the initial pressure and volume.
*   $P_2$ and $V_2$ are the final pressure and volume.

This law can also be related to density. Since volume ($V$) is mass ($m$) divided by density ($\rho$), we can write:

$P \left(\frac{m}{\rho}\right) = \text{constant}$

Which simplifies to:

$\frac{P}{\rho} = \text{constant}$ or $\frac{P_1}{\rho_1} = \frac{P_2}{\rho_2}$

## Charles's Law: Volume and Temperature

Charles's Law, attributed to Jacques Charles, describes the relationship between the volume and absolute temperature of a gas when the pressure and the amount of gas remain constant. It states that the volume of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute (Kelvin) temperature.

In simpler terms, if you heat a gas, its volume will expand, and if you cool it, its volume will contract, assuming the pressure stays the same.

The formula for Charles's Law is:

$V \propto T$

Or:

$\frac{V}{T} = \text{constant}$

For comparing two states, this becomes:

$\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$

Here, $T$ must always be in Kelvin. To convert Celsius to Kelvin, add 273.15.

Similar to Boyle's Law, Charles's Law can also be expressed in terms of density:

$\frac{m}{\rho T} = \text{constant}$

Which means:

$\rho T = \text{constant}$ or $\rho_1 T_1 = \rho_2 T_2$

## Gay-Lussac's Law: Pressure and Temperature

Gay-Lussac's Law, sometimes referred to as Amontons's Law, establishes the direct relationship between the pressure and absolute temperature of a gas when the volume and amount of gas are kept constant. It states that the pressure of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

This means that if you heat a gas in a rigid container, its pressure will increase, and if you cool it, its pressure will decrease.

Mathematically:

$P \propto T$

Or:

$\frac{P}{T} = \text{constant}$

For comparing two states:

$\frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2}$

Again, temperature ($T$) must be in Kelvin.

## The Ideal Gas Law: A Unified Approach

The Ideal Gas Law combines Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's laws into a single, comprehensive equation that describes the behavior of an ideal gas. An ideal gas is a theoretical gas composed of randomly moving point particles that do not interact with each other.

The Ideal Gas Law is given by:

$PV = nRT$

Where:
*   $P$ is the pressure of the gas.
*   $V$ is the volume of the gas.
*   $n$ is the number of moles of the gas.
*   $R$ is the universal gas constant (a constant value).
*   $T$ is the absolute temperature of the gas (in Kelvin).

This law is incredibly versatile and is widely used in various scientific and engineering applications. While no real gas is perfectly ideal, many gases behave very much like ideal gases under common conditions of temperature and pressure.

## Solved Problems Based on Gas Laws

Let's apply these laws to some practical examples.

**Problem 1: Boyle's Law**
A gas occupies a volume of 2.0 liters at a pressure of 1.5 atm. If the temperature remains constant, what will be the new volume if the pressure is increased to 3.0 atm?

*Given:*
$P_1 = 1.5 \text{ atm}$
$V_1 = 2.0 \text{ L}$
$P_2 = 3.0 \text{ atm}$

*Using Boyle's Law:*
$P_1V_1 = P_2V_2$
$(1.5 \text{ atm})(2.0 \text{ L}) = (3.0 \text{ atm})V_2$
$3.0 \text{ L·atm} = (3.0 \text{ atm})V_2$
$V_2 = \frac{3.0 \text{ L·atm}}{3.0 \text{ atm}} = 1.0 \text{ L}$

*Answer:* The new volume will be 1.0 L.

**Problem 2: Charles's Law**
A balloon contains 5.0 L of air at 27°C. If the pressure remains constant, what will be the volume of the balloon if the temperature is increased to 127°C?

*Given:*
$V_1 = 5.0 \text{ L}$
$T_1 = 27°C + 273.15 = 300.15 \text{ K}$
$T_2 = 127°C + 273.15 = 400.15 \text{ K}$

*Using Charles's Law:*
$\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2}$
$\frac{5.0 \text{ L}}{300.15 \text{ K}} = \frac{V_2}{400.15 \text{ K}}$
$V_2 = \frac{5.0 \text{ L} \times 400.15 \text{ K}}{300.15 \text{ K}}$
$V_2 \approx 6.67 \text{ L}$

*Answer:* The new volume will be approximately 6.67 L.

**Problem 3: Ideal Gas Law**
Calculate the number of moles of a gas contained in a 10.0 L tank at 25°C and 2.0 atm pressure. (R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K))

*Given:*
$V = 10.0 \text{ L}$
$T = 25°C + 273.15 = 298.15 \text{ K}$
$P = 2.0 \text{ atm}$
$R = 0.0821 \text{ L·atm/(mol·K)}$

*Using the Ideal Gas Law:*
$PV = nRT$
$n = \frac{PV}{RT}$
$n = \frac{(2.0 \text{ atm})(10.0 \text{ L})}{(0.0821 \text{ L·atm/(mol·K)})(298.15 \text{ K})}$
$n = \frac{20.0}{24.47} \text{ mol}$
$n \approx 0.817 \text{ mol}$

*Answer:* The tank contains approximately 0.817 moles of gas.
