Electrons are tiny, fundamental particles that play a huge role in how the world around us works. From the electricity that powers our homes to the chemical reactions that form everything we see, electrons are constantly at play, making things happen.

## Key takeaways

*   Electrons are fundamental subatomic particles with a negative electric charge.
*   They are incredibly small, having a mass significantly less than protons and neutrons.
*   Electrons exhibit both particle and wave-like properties, a concept known as wave-particle duality.
*   Their movement and arrangement are crucial for electricity, magnetism, and chemical bonding.

## What is an Electron?

An electron, symbolized as `e⁻`, is a fundamental subatomic particle carrying a single unit of negative electric charge. It's considered an elementary particle, meaning it has no known internal structure and isn't made up of smaller components. Electrons belong to the lepton family, one of the fundamental particle groups in physics.

While an electron's charge is equal in magnitude to that of a proton, its mass is vastly smaller – approximately 1/1836th the mass of a proton. In a neutral atom or molecule, the number of electrons perfectly balances the number of protons, ensuring an overall neutral charge. Electrons orbit the nucleus of an atom, held in place by electromagnetic forces.

Interestingly, electrons exhibit a dual nature: they can behave like discrete particles and also like waves. This wave-particle duality means they can collide like tiny balls but also show wave phenomena such as diffraction, similar to how light behaves.

## The Discovery of Electrons

The electron was discovered in 1897 by the British physicist J. J. Thomson through his groundbreaking cathode ray experiments. Before this, atoms were thought to be indivisible. Thomson's work changed this understanding forever.

### Thomson's Cathode Ray Experiment

Thomson conducted his experiments using a sealed glass tube, known as a discharge tube, which contained gas at a very low pressure and two electrodes: a cathode (negative) and an anode (positive). When a high voltage was applied across these electrodes, rays were observed originating from the cathode and moving towards the anode. These were named cathode rays.

Thomson made several key observations:

*   **Deflection by fields:** The cathode rays were deflected by both electric and magnetic fields. Crucially, they were always deflected towards the positive plate of an electric field, indicating they carried a negative charge.
*   **Independence of material:** The properties of these cathode rays, including their deflection, remained the same regardless of the type of gas in the tube or the material of the electrodes. This suggested that these negatively charged particles were a universal component of all atoms.

By carefully measuring the deflection of these rays, Thomson was able to calculate the charge-to-mass ratio (`e/m`) of these particles. His findings led him to conclude that these were not just rays, but streams of tiny, negatively charged particles – the first subatomic particle ever discovered, which he later named the electron.

## Properties of Electrons

Electrons possess several distinct properties that are fundamental to their role in physics and chemistry:

*   **Charge:** An electron carries a negative elementary electric charge. The magnitude of this charge is approximately `1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹` Coulombs.
*   **Mass:** The mass of an electron is extremely small, approximately `9.10938356 × 10⁻³¹` kilograms. This is about 1/1836th the mass of a proton.
*   **Spin:** Electrons have an intrinsic property called 'spin', which contributes to their magnetic properties.
*   **Wave-Particle Duality:** As mentioned, electrons exhibit characteristics of both particles and waves.
*   **Fundamental Particle:** Electrons are considered elementary particles, meaning they are not composed of smaller units.

Understanding electrons is vital for comprehending everything from how light bulbs work to the complex processes within a chemical reaction.
