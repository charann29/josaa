# Relation Between Kp And Kc - A Complete Guide

In chemical reactions involving gases, understanding equilibrium is crucial. Two key constants, Kp and Kc, help us describe this balance, one based on pressure and the other on concentration.

## Key Takeaways

*   **Kp and Kc are equilibrium constants** for reactions involving ideal gases, with Kp using partial pressures and Kc using molar concentrations.
*   **The ideal gas law** provides the fundamental link between pressure, volume, moles, and temperature, which is essential for deriving the relationship between Kp and Kc.
*   **Δn (delta n)** represents the change in the number of moles of gaseous products minus gaseous reactants, and it's a critical component in the Kp-Kc relationship.
*   **Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn** is the core equation connecting these two constants, where R is the gas constant and T is the absolute temperature.

## What are Kp and Kc?

When a reversible reaction involving gases reaches a state of equilibrium, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions become equal, and the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant. To quantify this state, we use equilibrium constants.

**Kc (Equilibrium Constant in terms of Concentration)**: This constant expresses the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations, each raised to the power of their stoichiometric coefficients, at equilibrium. Concentrations are typically given in moles per liter (molarity).

For a general reversible reaction:

aA(g) + bB(g) ⇌ cC(g) + dD(g)

The expression for Kc is:

`Kc = ([C]^c [D]^d) / ([A]^a [B]^b)`

Where [A], [B], [C], and [D] represent the molar concentrations of the respective gaseous species at equilibrium.

**Kp (Equilibrium Constant in terms of Partial Pressures)**: Similar to Kc, Kp describes the equilibrium state, but it uses the partial pressures of the gaseous reactants and products instead of their concentrations. Partial pressure refers to the pressure exerted by a single gas in a mixture of gases.

For the same general reaction:

aA(g) + bB(g) ⇌ cC(g) + dD(g)

The expression for Kp is:

`Kp = (P_C^c P_D^d) / (P_A^a P_B^b)`

Where P_A, P_B, P_C, and P_D are the partial pressures of the respective gaseous species at equilibrium.

## Deriving the Relationship Between Kp and Kc

The bridge between Kp and Kc lies in the ideal gas law, which relates pressure and concentration for gases. The ideal gas law is expressed as:

`PV = nRT`

Where:
*   `P` = pressure of the gas
*   `V` = volume of the gas
*   `n` = number of moles of the gas
*   `R` = ideal gas constant
*   `T` = absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

From this equation, we can rearrange to find an expression for pressure:

`P = (n/V)RT`

Notice that `n/V` is the molar concentration (moles per unit volume). So, we can write:

`P = [Concentration]RT`

Let's apply this to each gaseous component in our general reaction:

`P_A = [A]RT`
`P_B = [B]RT`
`P_C = [C]RT`
`P_D = [D]RT`

Now, substitute these expressions for partial pressures into the Kp equation:

`Kp = (( [C]RT )^c ( [D]RT )^d) / (( [A]RT )^a ( [B]RT )^b)`

Rearranging the terms, we group the concentration terms and the (RT) terms:

`Kp = ( [C]^c [D]^d / [A]^a [B]^b ) * ( (RT)^(c+d) / (RT)^(a+b) )`

The first part of this expression is simply Kc. For the second part, using exponent rules (`x^m / x^n = x^(m-n)`), we get:

`Kp = Kc * (RT)^((c+d) - (a+b))`

## Understanding Δn (Delta n)

The exponent `(c+d) - (a+b)` is a crucial term known as **Δn (delta n)**. It represents the change in the number of moles of gaseous species during the reaction.

`Δn = (Sum of stoichiometric coefficients of gaseous products) - (Sum of stoichiometric coefficients of gaseous reactants)`

So, the final relationship between Kp and Kc is:

`Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn`

## Special Cases for Δn

*   **If Δn = 0**: This occurs when the total number of moles of gaseous products is equal to the total number of moles of gaseous reactants. In this case, `(RT)^0 = 1`, so `Kp = Kc`.
*   **If Δn > 0**: The number of moles of gaseous products is greater than the number of moles of gaseous reactants. Then `Kp > Kc` (assuming RT > 1, which is typically true for standard conditions).
*   **If Δn < 0**: The number of moles of gaseous products is less than the number of moles of gaseous reactants. Then `Kp < Kc` (assuming RT > 1).

This relationship is fundamental in understanding gaseous equilibria and is often covered in Class 11 Chemistry curricula, as per NCERT guidelines.

Mastering the relationship between Kp and Kc is essential for advanced chemistry studies and competitive exams. Keep practicing with different reaction examples to solidify your understanding!
