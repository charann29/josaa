# Ideal Gas Equation: Equation, Derivation and Solved Examples

Gases behave in a simpler way than solids or liquids because their molecules are far apart and interact very little, except during collisions. An ideal gas is a theoretical gas whose molecules experience no forces other than those from collisions with each other and the container walls.

## Key Takeaways

*   The Ideal Gas Equation, PV = nRT, connects pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of an ideal gas.
*   This equation is derived by combining Boyle's Law, Charles's Law, and Avogadro's Law.
*   Understanding this concept is vital for Class 11 Physics and competitive exams like JEE Main and NEET.
*   The Universal Gas Constant (R) has a fixed value for all gases under standard conditions.

## Understanding the Ideal Gas Equation

The Ideal Gas Equation, also known as the equation of state, is a fundamental formula that describes the relationship between the pressure (P), volume (V), and absolute temperature (T) of a given amount of an ideal gas. This concept is a cornerstone of the Kinetic Theory of Gases, a significant chapter in Class 11 physics.

Its importance extends beyond board exams, being a frequently tested topic in competitive entrance examinations. For instance, NTA data shows that this concept has featured in approximately twelve questions in JEE Main over the past decade (2013-2023) and ten questions in NEET during the same period. It also appears in other engineering and medical entrance tests like SRMJEE, BITSAT, and WBJEE.

## Derivation of the Ideal Gas Equation

The Ideal Gas Equation is not an arbitrary formula; it's a powerful combination of three well-established gas laws:

### 1. Boyle's Law

This law states that for a fixed amount of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume. Mathematically, this can be expressed as:

$$P \propto \frac{1}{V} \quad \text{(at constant T and n)}$$

### 2. Charles's Law

Charles's Law explains that for a fixed amount of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to its absolute temperature. This is written as:

$$V \propto T \quad \text{(at constant P and n)}$$

### 3. Avogadro's Law

Avogadro's Law posits that for a fixed temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of moles (n) of the gas. This relationship is:

$$V \propto n \quad \text{(at constant P and T)}$$

### Combining the Laws

By bringing these three proportionalities together, we can state that:

$$V \propto \frac{nT}{P}$$

Rearranging this, we introduce a proportionality constant, R, known as the Universal Gas Constant, to form the Ideal Gas Equation:

$$PV = nRT$$

Where:

*   **P** = Pressure of the ideal gas
*   **V** = Volume of the ideal gas
*   **n** = Number of moles of the gas
*   **R** = Universal Gas Constant
*   **T** = Absolute temperature of the gas (in Kelvin)

## The Universal Gas Constant (R)

The Universal Gas Constant, R, is a fundamental physical constant that appears in the Ideal Gas Equation. Its value is consistent for all ideal gases under Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP) conditions. The approximate value of R is:

| Value of R | Units |
| :--------- | :---- |
| 8.314      | J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ |
| 0.0821     | L atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ |

Its dimensional formula is [ML²T⁻²N⁻¹Θ⁻¹].

## Boltzmann's Constant (k) and Specific Gas Constant (r)

While R is the universal gas constant per mole, there are other related constants:

*   **Boltzmann's Constant (k):** This constant relates the average kinetic energy of particles in a gas to the absolute temperature. It is effectively the gas constant per molecule, given by R divided by Avogadro's number (N_A).

    $$k = \frac{R}{N_A}$$

*   **Specific Gas Constant (r):** This constant is defined per unit mass of a gas, rather than per mole. It is calculated by dividing the Universal Gas Constant (R) by the molar mass (M) of the specific gas.

    $$r = \frac{R}{M}$$

    The unit for the specific gas constant is typically J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

## Solved Examples Based on the Ideal Gas Equation

Let's apply the Ideal Gas Equation to some practical scenarios.

### Example 1: Calculating Volume

**Question:** What volume will 2 moles of an ideal gas occupy at a pressure of 3 atm and a temperature of 300 K? (Use R = 0.0821 L atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)

**Solution:**

Given:

*   n = 2 moles
*   P = 3 atm
*   T = 300 K
*   R = 0.0821 L atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹

Using the Ideal Gas Equation, PV = nRT:

$$V = \frac{nRT}{P}$$

$$V = \frac{(2 \text{ mol})(0.0821 \text{ L atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹})(300 \text{ K})}{3 \text{ atm}}$$

$$V = \frac{49.26 \text{ L atm}}{3 \text{ atm}}$$

$$V = 16.42 \text{ L}$$

Therefore, the gas will occupy a volume of 16.42 liters.

### Example 2: Finding Temperature

**Question:** A container holds 0.5 moles of an ideal gas at a pressure of 2.5 x 10⁵ Pa and a volume of 5 x 10⁻³ m³. What is the temperature of the gas in Kelvin? (Use R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)

**Solution:**

Given:

*   n = 0.5 moles
*   P = 2.5 x 10⁵ Pa
*   V = 5 x 10⁻³ m³
*   R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹

Using the Ideal Gas Equation, PV = nRT:

$$T = \frac{PV}{nR}$$

$$T = \frac{(2.5 \times 10⁵ \text{ Pa})(5 \times 10⁻³ \text{ m³})}{(0.5 \text{ mol})(8.314 \text{ J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹})}$$

$$T = \frac{1250 \text{ J}}{4.157 \text{ J K⁻¹}}$$

$$T \approx 300.69 \text{ K}$$

Thus, the temperature of the gas is approximately 300.69 Kelvin.
