# Periodic Trends in Ionisation Enthalpy of Elements - Definition, Factors, Trends, FAQs

Ionisation enthalpy is a fundamental concept in chemistry, describing the energy needed to detach an electron from an atom. Understanding its patterns helps us predict how elements will behave chemically.

## Key Takeaways

*   Ionisation enthalpy is the energy required to remove an electron from an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state.
*   Successive ionisation enthalpies (second, third, etc.) are always higher than the first because removing electrons from an increasingly positive ion is harder.
*   Key factors influencing ionisation enthalpy are effective nuclear charge, atomic size, and electron shielding.
*   Across a period, ionisation enthalpy generally increases, while down a group, it typically decreases.

## What is Ionisation Enthalpy?

Ionisation enthalpy, often called ionisation energy, is the specific amount of energy an isolated atom, in its gaseous state and in its most stable electron configuration (ground state), needs to lose an electron. When an atom loses an electron, it transforms into a positively charged ion, known as a cation. The first ionisation enthalpy refers to the energy required to remove the first electron from one mole of such atoms.

For example, the removal of the first electron from a hydrogen atom can be represented as:
H (g) → H⁺ (g) + e⁻

### First, Second, and Subsequent Ionisation Enthalpies

After the first electron is removed, additional energy is needed to remove further electrons. The energy required to remove the second electron is called the second ionisation enthalpy, and so on. It's crucial to note that the second ionisation enthalpy is always greater than the first, and subsequent ones are even higher. This is because once an electron is removed, the remaining atom becomes a positive ion. Removing another electron from an already positively charged species requires more energy due to the increased attraction between the nucleus and the remaining electrons. For instance, an alkali metal readily loses its first electron to achieve a stable electron configuration, but removing a second electron would mean breaking into a stable, full shell closer to the nucleus, which demands significantly more energy.

## Factors Affecting Ionisation Enthalpy

Several factors determine how much energy is needed to remove an electron:

*   **Effective Nuclear Charge:** This is the net positive charge experienced by the outermost electrons. Inner electrons partially block the attraction of the nucleus for the outer electrons, a phenomenon known as the shielding effect. A higher effective nuclear charge means stronger attraction, thus higher ionisation enthalpy.
*   **Atomic Size (Atomic Radius):** As the atomic size increases, the outermost electrons are further from the nucleus. This reduces the attraction from the nucleus, making it easier to remove the electron and resulting in lower ionisation enthalpy.
*   **Electron Configuration Stability:** Atoms with stable electron configurations (like full or half-full subshells) tend to have higher ionisation enthalpies because extra energy is required to disrupt this stability.
*   **Penetration Effect:** Electrons in orbitals closer to the nucleus (e.g., s orbitals) are more strongly attracted than those in more diffuse orbitals (e.g., p orbitals) at the same principal energy level. Removing an s electron generally requires more energy than removing a p electron from the same shell.

## Periodic Trends in Ionisation Enthalpy

The periodic table beautifully illustrates systematic changes in ionisation enthalpy.

### Trends in Ionisation Enthalpy Down a Group

As you move down a group in the periodic table, the first ionisation enthalpy generally decreases. This is primarily due to two reasons:

1.  **Increased Atomic Size:** With each step down a group, a new electron shell is added, increasing the atomic radius. This places the outermost electrons further from the nucleus.
2.  **Enhanced Shielding Effect:** The increased number of inner electron shells effectively shields the outermost electrons from the nucleus's full positive charge. Both factors weaken the nuclear attraction on the valence electrons, making them easier to remove.

### Trends in Ionisation Enthalpy Across a Period

Moving from left to right across a period, the first ionisation enthalpy generally increases. Here's why:

1.  **Increased Effective Nuclear Charge:** As you move across a period, electrons are added to the same principal energy level, and the nuclear charge (number of protons) increases. The shielding effect from inner electrons remains relatively constant, leading to a higher effective nuclear charge experienced by the valence electrons.
2.  **Decreased Atomic Size:** The stronger attraction between the nucleus and the valence electrons pulls them closer, resulting in a smaller atomic radius.

These combined effects mean that valence electrons are held more tightly, requiring more energy to remove them. However, there are some notable exceptions to this general trend, such as the first ionisation enthalpy of Boron being lower than Beryllium, and Oxygen being lower than Nitrogen. These deviations are explained by the specific electron configurations and the stability associated with half-filled or fully-filled orbitals.

Understanding these trends is essential for predicting the chemical reactivity and bonding behaviour of elements.
