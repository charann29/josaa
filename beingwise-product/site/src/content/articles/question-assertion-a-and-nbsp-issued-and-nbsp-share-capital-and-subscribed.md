# Question: Assertion A: Issued share capital and subscribed share capital are always the same. Reason R: Subscribed capital is part of issued share capital. Thus it is always equal to the issued share capital. In the context of the above two statements, which of the follo...

Understanding the different types of share capital is fundamental for anyone studying business or finance. Let's clarify the relationship between issued share capital and subscribed share capital to see if the given statements hold true.

## Key Takeaways

*   Issued share capital refers to the total value of shares a company has offered to the public for subscription.
*   Subscribed share capital is the portion of the issued capital that investors have actually agreed to buy.
*   These two figures are not always identical; subscribed capital can be less than or equal to issued capital.
*   The reason provided in the assertion is also incorrect because subscribed capital is a *part* of issued capital, not necessarily always equal to it.

## Understanding Issued Share Capital

When a company decides to raise funds by selling shares, it first determines how many shares it wants to make available to the public. The total value of these shares, at their face value, is known as the issued share capital. It represents the maximum amount of capital the company has offered to potential investors from its authorized capital.

For example, if a company is authorized to issue 100,000 shares and decides to offer 50,000 shares to the public, then its issued share capital would be based on these 50,000 shares.

## Understanding Subscribed Share Capital

Subscribed share capital, on the other hand, refers to the actual value of shares that investors have committed to purchase from the issued capital. After a company issues shares, potential shareholders apply to buy them. The sum total of the face value of shares for which applications are accepted and allotted constitutes the subscribed capital.

It's crucial to note that it's possible for the public not to subscribe to all the shares a company has issued. In such cases, the subscribed capital will be less than the issued capital. If all issued shares are fully subscribed, then the two amounts would be equal.

## The Relationship Between Issued and Subscribed Capital

The fundamental difference lies in the 'offer' versus 'acceptance'. Issued capital is what the company offers, while subscribed capital is what the public accepts and commits to buying. Therefore, subscribed capital is a *part* of the issued share capital, but it is not always equal to it.

Consider this analogy: A bakery bakes 100 cakes (issued capital). Customers come and buy 80 of those cakes (subscribed capital). The number of cakes baked is not always the same as the number of cakes sold. The cakes sold are a part of the cakes baked. Similarly, subscribed capital is a part of issued capital.

## Analyzing the Assertion and Reason

Let's revisit the initial statements:

*   **Assertion A:** Issued share capital and subscribed share capital are always the same.
*   **Reason R:** Subscribed capital is part of issued share capital. Thus it is always equal to the issued share capital.

Based on our discussion:

*   **Assertion A is incorrect.** As explained, subscribed capital can be less than issued capital if not all shares are subscribed.
*   **Reason R is also incorrect.** While it correctly states that subscribed capital is *part* of issued share capital, it incorrectly concludes that this means they are *always equal*. Being a part does not automatically imply equality; it implies it can be less than or equal to the whole.

Therefore, both Assertion A and Reason R are not correct.

Understanding these distinctions is vital for accurate financial reporting and analysis, providing a clear picture of a company's capital structure.
