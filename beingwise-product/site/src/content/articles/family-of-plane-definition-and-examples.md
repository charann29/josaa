# Family of Plane: Definition and Examples

In mathematics, a plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely. Understanding how planes relate to each other, especially the concept of a "family of planes," is fundamental in three-dimensional geometry, a crucial part of Class 12 Mathematics.

## Key takeaways

*   A unique plane can be defined by three non-collinear points, two intersecting lines, or a line and a point not on it.
*   The concept of a "family of planes" helps describe multiple planes that share a common characteristic, such as being parallel or passing through a common intersection.
*   Three-dimensional geometry topics, including the family of planes, are vital for competitive exams like JEE Main, BITSAT, and WBJEE.
*   JEE Main has consistently featured questions on this topic, with a significant number appearing from 2013 to 2023.

## What Defines a Plane?

A single, specific plane can be uniquely identified in space in several ways. For instance, if you have three points that don't all lie on the same straight line, there's only one plane that can pass through all of them. Similarly, if you have two distinct lines that cross each other, they will both lie within exactly one plane. Another way to define a plane is by using a straight line and any point that is not on that line; these two elements together will determine a unique plane.

In practical applications, planes are used as reference surfaces for measurements, helping us calculate things like the circumference, area, and volume of objects.

## Understanding Parallel Planes

When we talk about planes that are parallel to each other, it means they never intersect. From a mathematical perspective, parallel planes share the same "normal vector." A normal vector is a line perpendicular to the plane. Therefore, if you have a plane defined by the vector equation $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n} = d_1$, any plane parallel to it will have the form $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n} = d_2$. Here, $\overrightarrow{n}$ is the same normal vector for both planes, and $d_2$ is a different constant that can be determined based on specific conditions.

In Cartesian coordinates, if the equation of a given plane is $ax + by + cz + d = 0$, then any plane parallel to it can be written as $ax + by + cz + k = 0$, where $k$ is any real number. The coefficients $a, b, c$ remain the same because they represent the direction of the normal vector, which is identical for parallel planes.

## Equation of a Plane Passing Through the Intersection of Two Planes (Vector Form)

Consider two planes, $\pi_1$ and $\pi_2$, with their equations in vector form as $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_1 = d_1$ and $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_2 = d_2$, respectively. The "family of planes" passing through the line where these two planes intersect can be represented by the equation:

$\overrightarrow{r} \cdot (\overrightarrow{n}_1 + \lambda \overrightarrow{n}_2) = d_1 + \lambda d_2$

Here, $\lambda$ (lambda) is a scalar constant. By changing the value of $\lambda$, you can represent any plane that passes through the common line of intersection of the original two planes. This equation essentially combines the conditions for both planes, ensuring that any point satisfying this combined equation also lies on the line where $\pi_1$ and $\pi_2$ meet.

## Derivation of the Equation for a Family of Planes

To understand why the above equation works, let's consider the derivation. Imagine two planes, $\pi_1$ and $\pi_2$, defined by $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_1 = d_1$ and $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_2 = d_2$. Any point that lies on the line where these two planes intersect must satisfy both equations simultaneously. Let $\overrightarrow{p}$ be the position vector of such a point $P$ on the intersection line. Therefore, we have:

1.  $\overrightarrow{p} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_1 = d_1$
2.  $\overrightarrow{p} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_2 = d_2$

Now, if we multiply the second equation by an arbitrary scalar $\lambda$ and add it to the first equation, we get:

$(\overrightarrow{p} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_1) + \lambda (\overrightarrow{p} \cdot \overrightarrow{n}_2) = d_1 + \lambda d_2$

Using the distributive property of the dot product, this simplifies to:

$\overrightarrow{p} \cdot (\overrightarrow{n}_1 + \lambda \overrightarrow{n}_2) = d_1 + \lambda d_2$

Since this holds true for any point $\overrightarrow{p}$ on the line of intersection, and $\overrightarrow{r}$ represents the position vector of a general point on a plane, the equation $\overrightarrow{r} \cdot (\overrightarrow{n}_1 + \lambda \overrightarrow{n}_2) = d_1 + \lambda d_2$ represents a plane ($\pi_3$) that passes through the intersection of $\pi_1$ and $\pi_2$. Each unique value of $\lambda$ defines a different plane within this family.

## Equation of a Plane Passing Through the Intersection of Two Planes (Cartesian Form)

If the two planes are given in Cartesian form as $a_1x + b_1y + c_1z + d_1 = 0$ and $a_2x + b_2y + c_2z + d_2 = 0$, then the equation of any plane passing through their intersection can be written as:

$(a_1x + b_1y + c_1z + d_1) + \lambda (a_2x + b_2y + c_2z + d_2) = 0$

Similar to the vector form, $\lambda$ is an arbitrary scalar constant. This equation provides a straightforward way to find the equation of a specific plane from the family if additional conditions (like passing through another point) are provided.

Mastering the concept of the family of planes is essential for excelling in Class 12 board exams and competitive entrance examinations like JEE Main, where it is a frequently tested topic. According to analysis, a significant number of questions on this topic have appeared in JEE Main between 2013 and 2023, highlighting its importance for aspiring engineers.
