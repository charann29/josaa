# Question: Nakul went to the market to buy groceries. He has some money with him, 20% of which he used to buy wheat flour and 25% of the remaining money is spent on cooking oils. If he is left with Rs. 900, then find out the total amount of money that Nakul had.

This problem is a classic example of percentage calculations often found in competitive exams and everyday financial planning. It helps us understand how to work with successive percentage deductions from an initial amount.

## Key Takeaways

*   **Understanding "Remaining Amount":** When a percentage is spent, the next percentage is calculated on what's left, not the original total.
*   **Step-by-Step Calculation:** Break down the problem into smaller, manageable steps to avoid errors.
*   **Algebraic Approach:** Using variables (like 'x' for the total amount) simplifies setting up the equation.
*   **Real-World Relevance:** These types of calculations are essential for budgeting, discounts, and understanding financial statements.

## Deciphering the Problem

The question asks us to find Nakul's initial sum of money, given his spending pattern and the final amount remaining. He first spends 20% on wheat flour, and then 25% of what was *left* on cooking oils. This distinction between spending from the total versus spending from the remaining amount is crucial.

## Setting Up the Equation

Let's assume the total amount of money Nakul had was 'X' rupees.

1.  **Spending on Wheat Flour:** Nakul used 20% of his money for wheat flour.
    *   Amount spent = 20% of X = (20/100) * X
    *   Amount remaining after wheat flour purchase = X - (20/100) * X = (80/100) * X

2.  **Spending on Cooking Oils:** He then spent 25% of the *remaining* money on cooking oils.
    *   The remaining money was (80/100) * X.
    *   Amount spent on cooking oils = 25% of [(80/100) * X] = (25/100) * (80/100) * X

3.  **Amount Left After All Purchases:** If 25% of the remaining amount was spent, then 75% of that remaining amount is what Nakul was left with.
    *   Amount left = 75% of [(80/100) * X] = (75/100) * (80/100) * X

We are told that Nakul is left with Rs. 900. So, we can set up the equation:

(75/100) * (80/100) * X = 900

## Solving for X

Now, let's solve this equation to find the total amount Nakul had:

1.  Simplify the fractions:
    *   (75/100) = 3/4
    *   (80/100) = 4/5

2.  Substitute these back into the equation:
    *   (3/4) * (4/5) * X = 900

3.  Multiply the fractions:
    *   (12/20) * X = 900
    *   (3/5) * X = 900

4.  Isolate X:
    *   X = 900 * (5/3)
    *   X = (900/3) * 5
    *   X = 300 * 5
    *   X = 1500

Therefore, the total amount of money Nakul had was Rs. 1,500.

## Alternative Approach: Working Backwards

Sometimes, it's easier to work backward from the final amount.

1.  **Before Cooking Oils:** Nakul was left with Rs. 900 after spending 25% of the *remaining* money on cooking oils. This means Rs. 900 represents 75% of the money he had *before* buying cooking oils.
    *   Let M be the money before buying cooking oils.
    *   75% of M = 900
    *   (75/100) * M = 900
    *   M = 900 * (100/75)
    *   M = 900 * (4/3)
    *   M = 1200
    So, Nakul had Rs. 1,200 before buying cooking oils.

2.  **Before Wheat Flour:** This Rs. 1,200 was the amount left after spending 20% of his *total* money on wheat flour. This means Rs. 1,200 represents 80% of his total initial money (X).
    *   80% of X = 1200
    *   (80/100) * X = 1200
    *   X = 1200 * (100/80)
    *   X = 1200 * (5/4)
    *   X = (1200/4) * 5
    *   X = 300 * 5
    *   X = 1500

Both methods confirm that Nakul initially had Rs. 1,500.

This exercise highlights the importance of careful reading and understanding the base on which percentages are calculated, a skill valuable for many competitive examinations and personal finance.
