Welcome to BeingWise! We understand that preparing for competitive exams often involves navigating tricky grammar rules. This guide breaks down a common English grammar question to help you master similar challenges and improve your overall language skills.

## Key takeaways

*   **Pronoun Consistency:** When a sentence begins with the indefinite pronoun "one," it must maintain consistency by using "one" or its possessive form, "one's," throughout.
*   **Avoid Switching Pronouns:** Do not switch to other pronouns like "he," "she," "his," or "her" when "one" is the initial subject.
*   **Possessive Form of "One":** The correct possessive form for "one" is "one's," indicating belonging or ownership.
*   **Common Error Type:** This type of error frequently appears in competitive exams to test a candidate's understanding of pronoun agreement and consistency.

## Understanding Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns refer to non-specific people or things. "One" is a classic example. When you use "one" as the subject of a sentence, it acts as a general placeholder for any person. For example, "One should always be polite" means that *any* person should always be polite.

## The Rule of Pronoun Agreement

The fundamental rule here is pronoun agreement. Once you establish a pronoun like "one" as your subject, any subsequent pronouns referring back to that subject must agree in number, gender, and *person*. In the case of "one," this means staying with "one" or its possessive form, "one's."

Consider the example: "One should keep his word."

*   The subject is "one" (indefinite pronoun).
*   The possessive pronoun used is "his."

Here, "his" refers to a male individual, but "one" is gender-neutral. This creates an inconsistency. To correct this, we must replace "his" with the possessive form of "one," which is "one's." The correct sentence becomes: "One should keep one's word."

## Why "His" is Incorrect

Using "his" after "one" is a common grammatical mistake because it introduces a specific gender into a general statement. While it might seem natural to use "his" or "her" in everyday conversation, formal English grammar, especially in competitive exams, requires strict adherence to pronoun consistency. The purpose of "one" is to make a general statement that applies to everyone, regardless of gender.

## Applying This to Other Scenarios

This rule extends to various sentence structures. For instance:

*   **Incorrect:** "One must finish his homework before playing."
*   **Correct:** "One must finish one's homework before playing."

*   **Incorrect:** "If one wants to succeed, he must work hard."
*   **Correct:** "If one wants to succeed, one must work hard."
