# Question: Assertion A: Issued share capital and subscribed share capital are always the same. Reason R: Subscribed capital is part of issued share capital. Thus it is always equal to the issued share capital. In the context of the above two statements, which of the follo...

Understanding the different types of share capital is fundamental for anyone looking to grasp how companies are financed and structured. Let's break down the relationship between issued share capital and subscribed share capital.

## Key Takeaways

*   Issued share capital refers to the total value of shares a company has offered to the public for subscription.
*   Subscribed share capital represents the value of shares that investors have actually agreed to purchase from the issued shares.
*   These two figures are often different, as not all shares offered for issue may be subscribed by the public.
*   Therefore, the assertion that they are always the same is incorrect, and the reason provided is also flawed.

## Understanding Share Capital

When a company is formed, it has an authorized share capital, which is the maximum amount of share capital it is legally permitted to issue. From this, the company decides how many shares it wants to offer to the public. This portion is known as issued share capital.

## What is Issued Share Capital?

Issued share capital is the part of the authorized capital that a company has actually offered to the public for subscription. It represents the value of shares that the company is willing to sell to investors. For example, if a company has an authorized capital of 100,000 shares but only offers 50,000 shares to the public, its issued share capital relates to those 50,000 shares.

## What is Subscribed Share Capital?

Subscribed share capital is the portion of the issued capital that investors have actually agreed to buy. It's the total value of shares for which shareholders have committed to pay. Following our previous example, if the company issued 50,000 shares but investors only agreed to buy 40,000 of them, then the subscribed share capital would be based on those 40,000 shares.

## Why They Are Not Always the Same

The key distinction lies in the difference between what is *offered* and what is *taken up*. A company might issue shares, but there's no guarantee that all of them will be subscribed by investors. Market demand, company performance, and other factors influence how many shares investors choose to buy. Thus, subscribed capital is indeed a part of issued capital, but it is not necessarily equal to it; it can be equal to or less than the issued capital.

## Conclusion

Both Assertion A and Reason R in the question are incorrect. Issued share capital and subscribed share capital are not always the same, and the reason provided misrepresents their relationship. Subscribed capital is a subset of issued capital, meaning it can be equal to or less than the issued capital, but not always equal.
